# Ohio Pre-Foreclosure & Wholesaling Legal Notes
# Project Velocity Compliance Reference

## IS WHOLESALING LEGAL IN OHIO?
**Yes.** Ohio does not have a specific statute prohibiting the assignment of real estate purchase contracts. The practice is legal as long as:
1. The contract includes an assignment clause
2. You don't misrepresent yourself as a licensed agent/broker
3. You have equitable interest (signed PSA) before marketing to buyers
4. You comply with all consumer protection and foreclosure-specific laws

## KEY OHIO STATUTES

### R.C. 1349.62 - Equity Purchasers (CRITICAL)
This is the big one for pre-foreclosure deals. Ohio defines an "equity purchaser" as someone who acquires residential property from an owner in foreclosure.

**Requirements:**
- Must provide the homeowner with a written "Notice of Cancellation" form
- Homeowner has a **5 business day right to cancel** any agreement
- Contract must be in writing and signed
- Must not take unconscionable advantage
- Must provide fair market value consideration

**What this means for us:**
- Our PSA must include the 5-day cancellation notice
- We cannot close faster than 5 business days after signing
- We must pay fair value (our MAO formula should ensure this)
- All terms must be clearly disclosed

### R.C. 5313.01-02 - Land Installment Contracts
Not directly applicable (we're doing assignment, not installment) but good to know.

### R.C. 4735 - Real Estate Licensing
- Wholesaling via assignment does NOT require a real estate license in Ohio
- You're selling your CONTRACT (personal property), not the real property itself
- DO NOT market the property itself - market the contract/deal
- If questioned, we are "disposing of our equitable interest"

## FORECLOSURE TIMELINE IN OHIO

Ohio is a **judicial foreclosure** state (court process required).

Typical timeline:
1. **Default** - Borrower misses payments (30-90 days)
2. **Notice of Default** - Lender sends formal notice
3. **Complaint Filed** - Lender files suit in Court of Common Pleas
4. **Service of Process** - Homeowner has 28 days to respond
5. **Summary Judgment** - If no response, 30-60 days after complaint
6. **Order of Sale** - Court orders Sheriff's sale
7. **Appraisal** - Three appraisers evaluate property
8. **Advertisement** - 4 Mondays before sale in Cincinnati Court Index
9. **Sheriff's Sale** - Online auction (Wednesdays at 11 AM)
10. **Confirmation** - 30 days post-sale, court confirms
11. **Deed Transfer** - To winning bidder

**Our window: Between steps 6-8 (after Order of Sale, before auction)**
This gives us 14-30 days typically. Our 7-day auction buffer gate ensures enough time.

## REDEMPTION PERIOD
Ohio allows the homeowner to **redeem (buy back) the property** at any time before the court's Confirmation Entry is signed. This means:
- Even after Sheriff's sale, the owner can redeem
- For our purposes: if we get a contract signed and assign it, we need to close BEFORE the Sheriff's sale
- Our assignment buyer must understand this timeline

## SMS/OUTREACH COMPLIANCE
- Ohio does NOT have a state-level "Do Not Call" registry (uses federal FTC list)
- TCPA applies to all outbound SMS/calls
- Must honor "Stop" requests immediately
- Pre-foreclosure solicitation is legal but:
  - Cannot be deceptive
  - Cannot create false urgency beyond actual deadlines
  - Must identify yourself
  - Cannot contact via SMS without prior express consent (TCPA gray area for property-specific messages)

**RISK MITIGATION:**
- Text first, don't call (lower TCPA exposure for property-specific info)
- Always identify as Brand/MGO Data LLC
- Always include opt-out language
- Keep records of all contacts
- Use dedicated number (not personal)

## RECORDING REQUIREMENTS
Ohio is a **one-party consent** state for call recording.
We can record our own calls without notifying the other party.

## ENTITY PROTECTION
- MGO Data LLC is our contracting entity
- Provides liability protection for Brand personally
- All contracts, earnest money, and assignments go through MGO Data LLC
- Keep personal and business accounts separate

## RED FLAGS (DO NOT PROCEED)
1. Homeowner appears mentally impaired or unable to understand the contract
2. Property has multiple liens totaling more than its value
3. Homeowner is represented by an attorney who objects
4. Any indication of elder abuse or exploitation
5. Homeowner is active military (SCRA protections apply)
6. HOA or municipal liens that could exceed our offer
