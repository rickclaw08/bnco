# Tax Planning Guide for ClawOps

## Overview

Taxes are unavoidable, but they're manageable if you plan ahead. This guide covers quarterly estimated taxes, deductible expenses for an AI agency, LLC vs sole proprietor considerations, and self-employment tax.

**Disclaimer:** I'm an AI CFO, not a licensed CPA or tax attorney. This is educational guidance. Consult a real tax professional for your specific situation.

---

## Part 1: Understanding Your Tax Obligations

### What Taxes You'll Pay as a Small Business

**1. Income Tax (Federal)**
- Tax on your business profit
- Rate depends on your total income (10% to 37% brackets)

**2. Self-Employment Tax**
- 15.3% on business profit (covers Social Security and Medicare)
- Applies to sole proprietors, single-member LLCs, and partners

**3. State Income Tax**
- Varies by state (0% in states like Texas, Florida, Nevada; up to 13.3% in California)

**4. Local Taxes**
- Some cities have local income or business taxes (rare for small businesses)

**Total effective rate:** Expect to pay 25-35% of your profit in taxes (federal income + self-employment + state).

## Part 2: Quarterly Estimated Taxes

### Why You Pay Quarterly

**IRS rule:** If you expect to owe $1,000+ in taxes for the year, you must pay estimated taxes quarterly.

**Why?** The IRS wants taxes withheld throughout the year (like W-2 employees). As a business owner, no one withholds for you, so YOU must pay quarterly.

**Penalty for not paying:** Underpayment penalty (typically 3-8% annual interest on what you owe). Not worth skipping.

---

### Quarterly Tax Deadlines (2026)

| Quarter | Income Period | Due Date |
|---------|---------------|----------|
| Q1 | Jan 1 - Mar 31 | April 15, 2026 |
| Q2 | Apr 1 - May 31 | June 16, 2026 |
| Q3 | Jun 1 - Aug 31 | September 15, 2026 |
| Q4 | Sep 1 - Dec 31 | January 15, 2027 |

**Set calendar reminders NOW.** Missing these costs you money.

---

### How to Calculate Quarterly Estimated Taxes

**Simple formula:**

1. Estimate your annual profit: Revenue minus expenses
2. Multiply by 30% (safe estimate for federal + self-employment + state)
3. Divide by 4 (quarterly payments)

**Example:**
- Expected annual profit: $60,000
- Total estimated tax: $60,000 × 0.30 = $18,000
- Quarterly payment: $18,000 ÷ 4 = $4,500

**Pay $4,500 per quarter.**

---

### More Accurate Calculation

**Federal Income Tax:**
- Use IRS tax brackets for your filing status
- Rough estimate: 15-25% of profit for most small business owners

**Self-Employment Tax:**
- 15.3% on 92.35% of net profit
- Formula: Net profit × 0.9235 × 0.153
- Example: $60,000 × 0.9235 × 0.153 = $8,478

**State Income Tax:**
- Check your state's rate (California: 9.3%, New York: 6.5%, Texas: 0%)

**Total estimated tax = Federal income tax + Self-employment tax + State income tax**

**Pro tip:** Use IRS Form 1040-ES worksheet (free) or hire an accountant to calculate exact amounts.

---

### How to Pay Quarterly Taxes

**Option 1: IRS Direct Pay (Free)**
1. Go to https://www.irs.gov/payments/direct-pay
2. Select "Estimated Tax" and tax year
3. Enter your SSN or EIN
4. Connect your bank account
5. Schedule payment

**Option 2: EFTPS (Electronic Federal Tax Payment System)**
1. Enroll at https://www.eftps.gov
2. Takes 5-7 days to activate
3. Schedule payments in advance (great for automation)

**Option 3: Credit Card**
- Pay via IRS-approved processors (payUSAtax, Pay1040, etc.)
- Convenience fee: 1.87-1.99% (avoid unless you need credit card points)

**Option 4: Mail a Check**
- Use IRS Form 1040-ES voucher
- Mail to IRS address for your state
- Slowest option, not recommended

**State taxes:** Check your state's tax website (e.g., California FTB, New York DTF). Most have online payment systems.

---

### Automating Quarterly Tax Savings

**Best practice: Set aside 30% of every dollar you earn.**

**How to automate:**

1. Open a separate savings account (call it "Tax Savings")
2. After each Stripe payout, transfer 30% to this account
3. Use these funds ONLY for quarterly tax payments

**Example:**
- Stripe payout: $2,000
- Transfer to Tax Savings: $600 (30%)
- Keep in Operating: $1,400

**By the end of the quarter, you'll have enough saved to pay taxes without stress.**

**Relay or Mercury make this easy:** Set up automatic percentage-based transfers after each deposit.

## Part 3: Deductible Expenses for an AI Agency

**Golden rule:** If it's "ordinary and necessary" for your business, it's deductible.

### Fully Deductible (100%)

**Software and subscriptions:**
- Stripe fees
- OpenClaw license
- CRM (HubSpot, Pipedrive)
- Project management (Notion, ClickUp, Asana)
- Communication tools (Slack, Zoom)
- AI tools (ChatGPT Plus, Claude Pro, API costs)
- Email marketing (Mailchimp, ConvertKit)
- Design tools (Canva, Figma)
- Hosting and domains (Vercel, Netlify, GoDaddy)

**Professional services:**

- Lawyer fees (contracts, business formation)
- Business coaching or consulting

**Marketing and advertising:**
- Google Ads, Facebook Ads, LinkedIn Ads
- SEO tools (Ahrefs, SEMrush)
- Content creation (freelance writers, designers)
- Website development

**Office expenses:**
- Coworking space membership
- Office supplies (notebooks, pens, printer ink)
- Business cards, branded materials

**Education:**
- Online courses (relevant to your business)
- Books (business, marketing, AI)
- Conference tickets
- Certifications

**Banking and finance:**
- Bank fees (monthly fees, wire transfer fees)
- Accounting software (QuickBooks, Wave, FreshBooks)
- Credit card processing fees

**Insurance:**
- Business liability insurance
- Professional liability (errors & omissions)
- Health insurance (if self-employed, special rules apply)

**Subcontractors:**
- Freelancers you hire (writers, designers, developers)
- VAs (virtual assistants)

**Equipment:**
- Computer, laptop, monitor, keyboard, mouse
- Phone (if used for business)
- Camera, microphone (for content creation)

**Internet and phone:**
- Business phone line (100%)
- Home internet (business use percentage, typically 50%)

**Travel:**
- Flights, hotels, rental cars (business trips only)
- Mileage (IRS rate: $0.67/mile in 2024)
- Parking and tolls
- Public transportation

---

### Partially Deductible (50%)

**Meals:**
- Client meals (50% deductible)
- Solo meals while traveling for business (50%)
- Team meals (50%)

**Entertainment:**
- Client entertainment (concerts, sports, etc.) is NO LONGER DEDUCTIBLE as of 2018 tax law change
- But meals during entertainment events (e.g., dinner at a game) are 50% deductible

---

### Home Office Deduction

**If you work from home, you can deduct a portion of:**
- Rent or mortgage interest
- Utilities (electricity, gas, water)
- Internet
- Renters or homeowners insurance
- Repairs and maintenance

**Two methods:**

**1. Simplified Method (Easier)**
- $5 per square foot of home office space
- Maximum 300 sq ft ($1,500 max deduction)
- No need to track actual expenses

**2. Regular Method (More accurate)**
- Calculate percentage of home used for business
- Apply that percentage to all home expenses
- Example: 150 sq ft office in 1,000 sq ft home = 15% deduction

**Requirements:**
- Space must be used EXCLUSIVELY for business (no dual-purpose rooms)
- Must be your principal place of business

**Most freelancers qualify.** If you work from a home office daily, take this deduction.

---

### What's NOT Deductible

**Personal expenses:**
- Personal meals (lunch at home)
- Personal travel (vacation flights)
- Personal car use (commuting)
- Gym memberships (unless you're a fitness business)
- Clothing (unless it's a uniform or branded workwear)

**Fines and penalties:**
- IRS penalties
- Traffic tickets
- Late fees on personal accounts

**Political contributions:**
- Donations to candidates or parties

**When in doubt, ask your accountant.** Don't guess.

## Part 4: LLC vs Sole Proprietor

### What's the Difference?

**Sole Proprietor:**
- Default business structure (no filing required)
- You and the business are legally the same entity
- Personal liability for business debts
- Profits taxed as personal income
- File Schedule C with your personal tax return (Form 1040)

**Single-Member LLC:**
- Legal entity separate from you (file articles of organization with your state)
- Limited liability protection (your personal assets protected from business lawsuits)
- Taxed the same as sole proprietor (profits pass through to personal return)
- File Schedule C (unless you elect S-Corp status)
- Costs $50-500 to form (depends on state)

**Multi-Member LLC:**
- Two or more owners
- Taxed as a partnership (file Form 1065)
- Each member reports their share on personal return

---

### LLC vs Sole Prop: Pros and Cons

| Feature | Sole Proprietor | Single-Member LLC |
|---------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Cost to start** | $0 (no filing) | $50-500 (state filing fee) |
| **Liability protection** | ❌ (personal assets at risk) | ✅ (personal assets protected) |
| **Tax filing** | Schedule C (simple) | Schedule C (same) or S-Corp |
| **Credibility** | Lower (you're "just a freelancer") | Higher (you're a "real business") |
| **Annual fees** | $0 | $0-800/year (varies by state) |
| **Bank account** | Personal account OK | Requires business account |
| **Complexity** | Super simple | Slightly more admin |

---

### Should You Form an LLC?

**Form an LLC if:**
- You're worried about liability (clients could sue you)
- You want to appear more professional
- You plan to hire employees
- You're making $50k+/year (more downside risk)

**Stay a sole proprietor if:**
- You're just starting (under $20k revenue)
- You want simplicity (no paperwork)
- You have strong insurance (professional liability covers you)

**Most AI agencies should form an LLC once they hit $30-50k annual revenue.**

---

### How to Form an LLC

**Step 1: Choose a state**
- Usually your home state (where you live and work)
- Delaware and Wyoming are popular for non-resident LLCs (but overkill for most)

**Step 2: Check name availability**
- Search your state's business registry
- Make sure "ClawOps LLC" isn't taken

**Step 3: File Articles of Organization**
- Go to your state's Secretary of State website
- Fill out the form (business name, address, registered agent)
- Pay filing fee ($50-500)

**Step 4: Get an EIN (Employer Identification Number)**
- Free from IRS: https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/apply-for-an-employer-identification-number-ein-online
- Takes 5 minutes
- You'll need this for business bank accounts and taxes

**Step 5: Create an Operating Agreement**
- Document outlining how your LLC operates
- Not required in all states, but highly recommended
- Templates available free online

**Step 6: Open a business bank account**
- Use your Articles of Organization and EIN
- See banking-setup.md for bank recommendations

**Step 7: File annual reports**
- Most states require annual or biennial reports ($0-100 fee)
- Set a calendar reminder

**Total time:** 1-2 hours
**Total cost:** $50-500 (one-time) + $0-100/year (annual reports)

---

### LLC Taxed as S-Corp (Advanced)

**What is it?**
- You elect to have your LLC taxed as an S-Corporation
- Allows you to split income into salary + distributions
- Can save on self-employment tax

**How it works:**
- Pay yourself a "reasonable salary" (subject to payroll taxes)
- Take remaining profit as distributions (NOT subject to self-employment tax)

**Example:**
- LLC profit: $100,000
- Reasonable salary: $50,000 (pay 15.3% self-employment tax = $7,650)
- Distribution: $50,000 (NO self-employment tax)
- **Tax savings:** ~$7,650 per year

**Downsides:**
- Requires payroll processing (costs $500-1,500/year)
- More paperwork (file Form 1120-S)
- Only worth it at higher income levels ($60k+ profit)

**When to do it:** Once you're consistently making $80k+ profit per year. Consult a CPA to set it up.

## Part 5: Self-Employment Tax Deep Dive

### What is Self-Employment Tax?

**Rate:** 15.3% of net profit

**Breakdown:**
- 12.4% Social Security (on first $168,600 of income in 2024)
- 2.9% Medicare (on all income)
- 0.9% Additional Medicare (on income over $200,000)

**Who pays it:** Sole proprietors, single-member LLCs, partners, freelancers

**W-2 employees pay half (7.65%), employer pays half (7.65%). You pay both halves.**

---

### How to Calculate Self-Employment Tax

**Formula:**
1. Net profit = Revenue minus expenses
2. Taxable profit = Net profit × 0.9235 (you get a small deduction)
3. Self-employment tax = Taxable profit × 0.153

**Example:**
- Net profit: $60,000
- Taxable profit: $60,000 × 0.9235 = $55,410
- Self-employment tax: $55,410 × 0.153 = $8,478

**You'll pay $8,478 in self-employment tax on top of income tax.**

---

### How to Reduce Self-Employment Tax

**1. Maximize deductions**
- The lower your net profit, the lower your self-employment tax
- Track EVERY deductible expense

**2. Form an S-Corp (advanced)**
- Split income into salary + distributions
- Only salary is subject to self-employment tax
- Requires payroll and more admin

**3. Contribute to retirement accounts**
- Solo 401(k) or SEP IRA contributions reduce taxable income
- Not a direct self-employment tax savings, but lowers overall tax

**4. Hire a CPA**
- A good accountant can find deductions you missed
- Often pays for itself in tax savings

## Part 6: Tax Planning Strategies

### Monthly Tax Routine

**Every month:**
1. Categorize all expenses in accounting software
2. Calculate profit for the month
3. Transfer 30% of profit to tax savings account
4. Review deductions: Did I miss anything?

**Quarterly (before estimated tax due date):**
1. Generate profit & loss statement
2. Calculate total tax owed (income + self-employment + state)
3. Pay estimated taxes online
4. Update tax tracker (how much paid vs. expected annual)

**Annually (January-April):**
1. Gather all tax documents (1099s, receipts, mileage log)
2. File tax return (Form 1040 + Schedule C)
3. Pay any remaining balance or claim refund
4. Adjust quarterly payments for next year based on actual income

---

### Tax Deduction Tracking

**Best practices:**
1. Use accounting software (Wave, QuickBooks, FreshBooks) to auto-categorize expenses
2. Scan receipts with mobile app (photo = digital record)
3. Track mileage with app (MileIQ, Everlance, or pen and paper)
4. Keep records for 7 years (IRS audit period)

**Monthly deduction checklist:**
- [ ] All software subscriptions categorized
- [ ] Stripe fees tracked
- [ ] Client meals logged (50% deductible)
- [ ] Mileage logged (if any)
- [ ] Home office percentage calculated (if applicable)
- [ ] Equipment purchases recorded
- [ ] Receipts scanned and attached

---

### Year-End Tax Planning

**Before December 31:**

**1. Prepay expenses (if it helps)**
- Pay for January's software subscriptions in December (deduct this year)
- Buy equipment you need (immediate deduction up to $1,220,000 via Section 179)

**2. Defer income (if it helps)**
- Delay invoicing until January (income counted in next year)
- Only do this if you expect lower income next year

**3. Maximize retirement contributions**
- Solo 401(k): Up to $69,000 for 2024 (or 100% of income if lower)
- SEP IRA: Up to 25% of net self-employment income
- Contributions reduce taxable income

**4. Review estimated tax payments**
- Did you pay enough? If not, pay Q4 by January 15 to avoid penalties

**5. Charitable donations**
- Donate to legitimate nonprofits (deductible if you itemize)

**Consult your CPA in November to optimize year-end moves.**

## Part 7: Hiring a Tax Professional

### When to Hire a CPA

**You should hire a CPA if:**
- Your business makes $50k+/year
- You formed an LLC or S-Corp
- You have multiple income streams
- You're confused or stressed about taxes
- You were audited or received an IRS notice

**Cost:** $500-2,000 for tax prep (depends on complexity)

**Worth it?** Almost always yes. A good CPA finds deductions that pay for their fee.

---

### What to Look for in a CPA

**Must-haves:**
- Experience with small business taxes
- Familiar with your industry (service business, digital, freelance)
- Licensed CPA (not just a tax preparer)
- Good communication (answers your questions in plain English)

**Where to find:**
- Ask other small business owners for referrals
- Search "small business CPA [your city]"
- Use platforms like Bench or Pilot (online accounting + tax services)

**Questions to ask:**
- How many small business clients do you have?
- What's your fee structure?
- Do you handle quarterly estimated taxes or just annual filing?
- Will you represent me if I'm audited?

---

### DIY vs CPA

**DIY with TurboTax or H&R Block:**
- Cost: $100-200
- Good for: Simple sole proprietor, under $30k revenue
- Risk: You might miss deductions

**Hire a CPA:**
- Cost: $500-2,000
- Good for: LLC, S-Corp, $50k+ revenue, complex finances
- Benefit: Expert advice, audit support, peace of mind

**Morgan's recommendation:** DIY for year 1 if revenue is under $30k. Hire a CPA by year 2.

## Part 9: Common Tax Mistakes to Avoid

**1. Not paying quarterly taxes**
- Penalty: 3-8% interest on what you owe
- Solution: Set reminders, automate savings

**2. Mixing personal and business expenses**
- Makes accounting a nightmare, increases audit risk
- Solution: Separate bank accounts, separate credit cards

**3. Missing deductions**
- Leaving money on the table
- Solution: Track everything, hire a CPA

**4. Not keeping receipts**
- Can't prove expenses if audited
- Solution: Scan receipts immediately, use accounting software

**5. Forgetting about self-employment tax**
- Surprise bill at tax time
- Solution: Calculate 30% total tax (income + self-employment + state)

**6. Claiming personal expenses as business**
- Audit red flag, potential fraud
- Solution: Only deduct legitimate business expenses

**7. Not filing on time**
- Late filing penalties (5% per month, up to 25%)
- Solution: File by April 15 or file extension (Form 4868)
