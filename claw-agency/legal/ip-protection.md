# Intellectual Property Protection Guide

Strategies to protect your AI automation agency's name, brand, frameworks, and client work.

---

## 1. Trademark Protection (Agency Name & Brand)

### What to Trademark
- **Agency name** (word mark)
- **Logo** (design mark)
- **Tagline/slogan** (if distinctive)

### Steps to Register a Federal Trademark (USPTO)

1. **Search for conflicts**
   - USPTO TESS database: [tess2.uspto.gov](https://tess2.uspto.gov)
   - Search for identical and similar marks in your service class
   - Also search state databases, domain names, and social media

2. **Determine your filing basis**
   - **Use in commerce:** You're already using the mark commercially
   - **Intent to use:** You plan to use it (additional steps and fees)

3. **Select the right class(es)**
   - **Class 35:** Advertising, business management, business consulting
   - **Class 42:** Scientific/technological services, software development, IT consulting
   - File in both if applicable

4. **File the application**
   - Online via [USPTO TEAS](https://www.uspto.gov/trademarks/apply)
   - **Cost:** $250–$350 per class (TEAS Plus: $250, TEAS Standard: $350)
   - Total for 2 classes: $500–$700

5. **Timeline**
   - Filing to registration: **8–12 months** (if no opposition)
   - Examination: 3-4 months after filing
   - Publication for opposition: 30 days
   - Registration: 2-3 months after publication

6. **Maintain your trademark**
   - File **Section 8 Declaration** (years 5-6)
   - File **Section 9 Renewal** (every 10 years)
   - **Use it or lose it** — must actively use the mark in commerce

### Immediate Protection (Before Registration)
- Use **™** symbol immediately (no registration required)
- Switch to **®** after federal registration
- Document your first use date and keep evidence (screenshots, invoices, contracts)

### Cost Summary
| Item | Cost |
|------|------|
| USPTO filing (per class) | $250–$350 |
| Attorney assistance | $1,000–$2,500 |
| DIY (2 classes) | $500–$700 |
| Maintenance (years 5-6) | $225–$425/class |
| Renewal (every 10 years) | $300–$500/class |

## 2. Protecting Your Frameworks & Tools

### Trade Secrets (Primary Strategy)
Your internal AI frameworks, prompts, automation templates, and methodologies are best protected as **trade secrets**.

**Requirements to maintain trade secret status:**
- Information has economic value because it's not publicly known
- You take **reasonable steps** to keep it secret

**Reasonable steps include:**
- ✅ Confidentiality clauses in all contracts (MSA Section 6)
- ✅ NDAs with employees, contractors, and partners
- ✅ Access controls — limit who can access proprietary code/prompts
- ✅ Label documents as "CONFIDENTIAL" or "PROPRIETARY"
- ✅ Use private repositories (never public GitHub for proprietary work)
- ✅ Employee/contractor IP assignment agreements
- ✅ Exit procedures — revoke access immediately upon departure
- ✅ Document your trade secrets in an internal registry

**Legal protection:**
- **Defend Trade Secrets Act (DTSA):** Federal law providing civil remedies for trade secret misappropriation
- **State laws:** Most states follow the Uniform Trade Secrets Act (UTSA)
- No registration required — protection is automatic if you maintain secrecy

### Copyright (Automatic Protection)
- Your code, documentation, training materials, and written content are **automatically copyrighted** upon creation
- No registration required for protection to exist
- **Registration benefits ($45–$65 per work via copyright.gov):**
  - Required before filing a lawsuit (US works)
  - Enables statutory damages ($750–$150,000 per infringement) and attorney's fees
  - Creates a public record

**What to register:** Key frameworks, proprietary software, major documentation — not every small script.

### Patents (Rarely Worth It for Agencies)
- Software patents are expensive ($10,000–$25,000+), slow (2-4 years), and difficult to enforce
- **Not recommended** for most agencies unless you've invented a truly novel technical process
- Consider a **provisional patent** ($320 filing fee) if you believe you have something patentable — gives you 12 months to decide

## 3. Protecting Client Work

### Contractual Protection (Already Built Into Your MSA)
Your MSA (Section 5) establishes:
- Client owns custom deliverables (after full payment)
- You retain your tools and frameworks
- License granted for Company Materials embedded in deliverables

### Additional Safeguards
- **Separate client environments** — never mix client code/data between projects
- **Version control** — maintain clear git history showing what you built vs. what's your pre-existing IP
- **Deliverable documentation** — clearly identify what's custom (client-owned) vs. framework (company-owned) in each delivery
- **Data handling procedures:**
  - Encrypt client data at rest and in transit
  - Delete client data within 30 days of project completion (unless retention agreed)
  - Never use one client's proprietary data to benefit another client

## 4. Contractor & Employee IP Agreements

Anyone who creates IP for your agency must sign an agreement covering:

### Key Provisions
1. **Work-for-hire declaration** — all work created within scope of engagement is owned by the Company
2. **IP assignment** — explicit assignment of all rights (work-for-hire doctrine doesn't cover all situations)
3. **Pre-existing IP disclosure** — contractor lists any pre-existing tools they'll use
4. **Non-compete** (where enforceable) — limited in scope and duration
5. **Non-solicitation** — can't poach your clients for 12-24 months
6. **Confidentiality** — survives the engagement
7. **Return of materials** — all company materials returned/deleted upon termination

### Template Clause (IP Assignment)
> *Contractor hereby irrevocably assigns to the Company all right, title, and interest in and to all Work Product, including all intellectual property rights therein. "Work Product" means all inventions, works of authorship, code, designs, documentation, and other materials created by Contractor in the course of performing services under this Agreement.*

## 5. Domain & Digital Asset Protection

- **Register your domain** in all major TLDs: .com, .io, .ai, .co
- **Register social media handles** across all major platforms (even if not active yet)
- **WHOIS privacy** — enable on all domains to protect personal information
- **Brand monitoring:**
  - Google Alerts for your agency name
  - USPTO monitoring for similar trademark filings
  - Domain monitoring for typosquatting

## 6. Enforcement Strategy

If someone infringes your IP:

### Escalation Steps
1. **Document the infringement** — screenshots, URLs, timestamps
2. **Cease and desist letter** — formal demand to stop (can often be DIY or attorney-drafted for $300–$500)
3. **DMCA takedown** — for online copyright infringement (free, effective for web content)
4. **Platform reports** — report to GitHub, social media, app stores, etc.
5. **Formal legal action** — if above steps fail, engage an IP attorney

### When to Involve an Attorney
- Trademark opposition proceedings
- Suspected trade secret theft
- Patent infringement claims
- Significant revenue at risk
