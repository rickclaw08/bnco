# Objection Handling Guide - ClawOps

## Core Principle
**Objections are requests for more information, not rejection.** Your job is to understand the real concern, validate it, and address it honestly.

**Framework: Feel, Felt, Found**
1. "I understand how you feel..."
2. "Other clients felt the same way..."
3. "What they found was..."

---

## Common Objections & Responses

### 1. "It's too expensive" / "We don't have the budget"

**Understand the real concern:**
"I hear you. Can I ask, is it that the number itself is outside your range, or are you not seeing how this pays for itself?"

**If it's about ROI:**
"Totally fair. Let's do the math together. You mentioned [task] takes [X hours per week]. At $[their hourly rate or team cost], that's $[monthly cost] just in time. Our system costs $[price] and handles that plus [other tasks]. You're actually saving $[amount] per month, not spending."

**If it's truly budget:**
"I get it, budgets are real. Here's what I'd suggest: We have a starter package at $500/month that focuses on just [their #1 pain point]. You see results there, then we expand. Or we can revisit this in [3 months] when you have more runway. Does either of those work?"

**Reframe:**
"I'd actually argue you can't afford NOT to do this. Every month you wait, you're losing $[calculated cost] in time and $[estimated revenue] in opportunities. That's real money walking out the door."

---

### 2. "I need to think about it"

**Uncover the real objection:**
"Of course, this is an important decision. Can I ask what specifically you want to think through? Is it the investment, the timing, how it would work with your team, or something else?"

*[They'll usually reveal the real concern]*

**Address directly:**
"Got it. Let's talk through [their concern] right now. What questions do you have about that?"

**Create urgency (if genuine):**
"I totally respect that. The only thing I'd mention is that every week you think about it, you're still dealing with [their pain point]. If we start next week, you could have relief by [specific date]. What would help you make a decision faster?"

**Set a specific follow-up:**
"No problem at all. How about this: Take a few days, talk to [spouse/partner/team], and let's reconnect on [specific day/time]. I'll send you [specific resource] to help with your decision. Does Thursday at 2pm work?"

---

### 3. "We're not technical enough" / "Our team won't use it"

**Validate and reassure:**
"I'm glad you brought that up. Here's the thing: Our best clients aren't technical at all. That's exactly why they hire us. We build it, we set it up, we train your team, and we support you. You don't need to know how it works, just how to use it."

**Provide proof:**
"One of our clients is a 60-year-old real estate agent who barely uses email. She's now running automated lead follow-ups like a pro because we made it simple. If she can do it, your team definitely can."

**Offer safety net:**
"And if anyone gets stuck, we're a text or call away. We don't just build and disappear. This is a partnership."

---

### 4. "We tried automation before and it didn't work"

**Acknowledge and differentiate:**
"I hear that a lot, actually. Can you tell me what you tried and what went wrong?"

*[Listen for: DIY tools, offshore developers, overpromised and underdelivered, too complex]*

**Respond based on their experience:**

**If DIY tools (Zapier, etc.):**
"Yeah, those tools are great for simple stuff, but they break easily and require constant babysitting. What we build is custom for your exact workflow, and we maintain it. You're not the IT person anymore."

**If bad developer experience:**
"That's frustrating. The difference here is we're not just developers. We understand business operations. We're not building what sounds cool, we're building what actually saves you time and makes you money."

**If it was too complex:**
"That's a common mistake. A lot of agencies overbuild. We start small, one bottleneck at a time, get it working perfectly, then expand. You see value fast, not six months from now."

**Provide assurance:**
"Look, I can't undo your past experience, but I can promise you this: We start with a pilot, you see results before you commit to anything bigger, and if it's not working, we fix it or refund you. Fair?"

---

### 5. "I need to talk to my [partner/team/spouse]"

**Qualify the situation:**
"Absolutely, makes sense. Quick question: If they're on board, is this something you'd want to move forward with?"

**If yes:**
"Great. What concerns do you think they'll have? Let's make sure you have good answers for them."

*[Prep them to sell internally]*

**Offer to join the conversation:**
"Would it help if I joined that conversation? I'm happy to hop on a quick call with you and [person] to answer their questions directly. That way nothing gets lost in translation."

**If no (they're the real decision-maker stalling):**
"Of course. Take your time. What specifically do you want to discuss with them? Maybe I can provide some info that helps that conversation."

*[This usually reveals the real objection]*

---

### 6. "We're too busy right now" / "Bad timing"

**Empathize and flip it:**
"I totally get it. You're busy. But can I challenge that for a second? The reason you're too busy is exactly why you need this. Automation gives you time back so you're not drowning."

**Create a timeline:**
"Here's what I'd suggest: We do the heavy lifting. You give us maybe 2-3 hours over the next two weeks to map out your process, and we build everything. Then you're LESS busy going forward. Does that work?"

**Alternative approach:**
"What if we just handle [one small thing] right now while you're busy, prove it works, and then tackle the bigger stuff when you have bandwidth? At least you get some relief immediately."

**Reality check:**
"I hear you, but let me ask: When will you NOT be busy? If the answer is 'never,' then we need to solve this now, not wait for a magical slow period that won't come."

---

### 7. "Can you send me a proposal?"

**This is often a polite brush-off. Qualify first:**

**"Of course, I can send that over. Before I do, let me make sure I'm sending the right thing. If the proposal addresses [their pain points] and the investment makes sense, is this something you'd move forward with?"**

**If yes:**
"Perfect. I'll get that to you by [tomorrow/end of day]. Let's schedule 20 minutes [specific time] to walk through it together so I can answer questions. Work for you?"

*[Never send a proposal without a follow-up meeting scheduled]*

**If no/hesitation:**
"Okay, I want to be respectful of both our time. It sounds like maybe this isn't the right fit or timing. What's really holding you back?"

*[Get to the real objection]*

---

### 8. "We'll just hire someone to do this"

**Acknowledge and compare:**
"You definitely could. Let's talk through that. A junior person to handle [tasks] is probably $40-50K per year plus benefits, so call it $60K all-in. They need training, management, they take PTO, they might quit."

**Show the math:**
"Our Growth package is $24K per year, works 24/7, never calls in sick, never needs a raise, and handles more volume than one person could. Plus, you're not managing an employee."

**Hybrid approach:**
"Or here's another way to think about it: Use us for the repetitive stuff that doesn't need human judgment, and hire a person for the high-value work. You get more leverage with the same budget."

**Ask the real question:**
"Is hiring someone really on the table right now, or does this feel like less commitment than bringing on an employee?"

---

### 9. "How do I know it will work for us?"

**Validate concern:**
"That's the right question to ask. You shouldn't take my word for it."

**Provide proof:**
"Here's what I'd suggest: We do a pilot focused on [their #1 pain point]. 2-3 weeks, fixed cost, specific outcome. You see it working in your business before you commit to anything bigger. If it doesn't deliver, you walk away and you're only out [pilot cost]. Fair?"

**Social proof:**
"We've done this exact thing for [similar business type]. They were skeptical too. After 30 days, they were saving [X hours/week] and [specific result]. I can connect you with them if you want to hear it firsthand."

**Risk reversal:**
"And look, we have a 30-day money-back guarantee. If you're not seeing value, you get your money back. The only risk is staying where you are and nothing changing."

---

### 10. "We're already using [tool/platform]"

**Don't compete, complement:**
"That's great! [Tool] is solid for [what it does]. What we do is different. We custom-build automation that connects your tools together and handles the stuff [tool] doesn't do."

**Example:**
"So you're using [CRM]? Perfect. We can automate data entry into it, trigger follow-ups based on behavior, pull reports automatically. We make [CRM] work harder for you without you doing more work."

**Identify gaps:**
"Can I ask: What are you still doing manually even though you have [tool]?"

*[This reveals where their current solution falls short]*

**Position as integration:**
"We're not replacing [tool], we're making it 10x more powerful by connecting it to everything else and automating the workflows around it."

---

### 11. "I need to see case studies / references"

**Provide immediately:**
"Absolutely. I'll send you three case studies from [similar industries] right after this call. They show the exact problems, what we built, and the results."

**Offer direct contact:**
"Even better, I can connect you with [Client Name] who runs a [similar business]. They're happy to chat about their experience. Would that help?"

**Ask what they need to see:**
"What specifically would make you feel confident? Is it ROI numbers, technical specs, or just hearing from someone like you who took the leap?"

---

### 12. "What if we outgrow this or need changes?"

**This is actually a buying signal. They're imagining using it.**

**Reassure:**
"Great question. Everything we build is flexible. As your business evolves, we update the automation. That's included in your monthly fee. You're never locked into something that doesn't fit anymore."

**Growth is good:**
"And honestly, outgrowing it is a GOOD problem. That means business is booming. We'll scale with you. Some clients started at $500/month and now they're at $5K because they've grown so much."

**Commitment level:**
"You're not signing a lifetime contract. Month-to-month after the first 90 days. If it's not working, you can walk away. But I'm confident you'll want to expand, not leave."

## Objection Handling Principles

### 1. Never argue or get defensive
The moment you argue, you lose. Stay curious and collaborative.

### 2. Isolate the objection
"If we solved [objection], is there anything else holding you back?" 
Make sure you're addressing the REAL concern, not surface-level stuff.

### 3. Use their words
If they say "overwhelmed," use "overwhelmed" in your response. Don't translate to your jargon.

### 4. Silence is powerful
After you respond to an objection, SHUT UP. Let them process. Don't fill the silence.

### 5. Some people aren't ready, and that's okay
Not every objection can be overcome. If they're truly not a fit, let them go gracefully and stay in touch.

---

## When to Walk Away

Sometimes the best sales move is to NOT close the deal.

**Walk away when:**
- They don't have a real pain point (just looking around)
- Budget is genuinely not there and won't be soon
- They want you to solve problems you can't solve
- They're difficult, demanding, or disrespectful
- Your gut says this will be a nightmare client

**How to walk away gracefully:**
"I really appreciate your time, [Name]. Based on what we've discussed, I don't think we're the right fit right now. I don't want to take your money if I'm not confident we can deliver amazing results. Let's stay in touch, and maybe things will align down the road."

**Why this works:**
- You protect your reputation
- You free up time for better prospects
- You show integrity (which sometimes makes them want you MORE)
- You avoid nightmare clients

## Role-Playing Practice

**Practice these objections with a colleague:**
1. Record yourself responding
2. Listen back (cringe optional)
3. Identify filler words, weak language, or defensive tones
4. Iterate until it feels natural

**The goal:** Sound like a human having a conversation, not a salesperson with canned responses.
