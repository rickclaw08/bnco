## 2. Emergency Protocols

### TRUE EMERGENCIES - Tell caller to call 911 FIRST, then call us back:
- Gas leak (rotten egg smell) - Evacuate immediately, do not flip switches
- Carbon monoxide alarm going off - Evacuate immediately
- Electrical fire or burning smell from HVAC unit - Evacuate, call fire department
- Furnace producing visible flames outside the unit

### URGENT (Same-Day/Next-Day Service Needed):
- No heat when temperatures are below freezing (risk of pipe freeze)
- No AC when temperatures are above 100F, especially with elderly, infants, or medical conditions
- Water leaking from HVAC unit causing active property damage
- Unusual loud banging, screeching, or grinding from unit
- System running continuously without reaching set temperature (significant gap)

### ROUTINE (Schedule Within Normal Business Hours):
- AC not cooling as well as it used to
- Strange but intermittent noises
- Thermostat not responding correctly
- Uneven temperatures between rooms
- Higher than normal energy bills
- Musty or dusty odor from vents

### Triage Questions to Ask:
1. "Is there any smell of gas or a rotten egg odor?" (If yes, tell them to evacuate and call 911)
2. "Is your carbon monoxide detector going off?" (If yes, tell them to evacuate and call 911)
3. "Is your system completely not working, or is it running but not performing well?"
4. "How old is your system?" (Helps tech prepare)
5. "What is the current temperature inside your home?"
6. "Are there any elderly people, young children, or anyone with medical conditions in the home?"
7. "Have you checked your thermostat settings and your air filter?"
8. "Have you checked that the breaker hasn't tripped?"
9. "When was your system last serviced?"

### Safety Instructions for Callers:
- Gas smell: "Leave the house immediately. Do not turn on or off any lights or appliances. Call 911 from outside. Once you are safe, call us back."
- CO alarm: "Get everyone out of the house right now, including pets. Call 911. Do not go back inside."
- No heat in freezing temps: "While you wait for our technician, open cabinet doors under sinks to help prevent pipes from freezing. If you have a space heater, use it in one room and keep doors closed to retain heat. Do not use your oven or stove for heating."
- Water leaking: "If you can safely reach the HVAC unit, try to turn it off at the thermostat. Place towels or a bucket under the leak."

- **Winter (December-February):** Replace filter every 1-2 months, check for ice buildup on heat pump outdoor units

## 4. Industry Terminology Glossary

### System Types
- **Central Air (Central AC):** A cooling system that distributes cold air through ductwork from a central unit. Most common in US homes.
- **Split System:** The standard setup - one outdoor unit (condenser) and one indoor unit (evaporator/air handler). Most homes have this.
- **Package Unit:** All components in one outdoor cabinet. Common in areas with crawl spaces or no basements.
- **Heat Pump:** A system that both heats and cools by transferring heat. Uses refrigerant. More efficient than furnaces in mild climates.
- **Ductless Mini-Split:** A system without ductwork. An outdoor unit connects to one or more wall-mounted indoor units via refrigerant lines.
- **Furnace:** A heating unit that burns fuel (gas, oil, propane) or uses electric resistance to produce heat. Distributes heat through ductwork.
- **Boiler:** Heats water to provide heat through radiators or radiant floor systems. Not the same as a furnace.
- **Geothermal:** A heat pump system that exchanges heat with the ground rather than outdoor air. Very efficient but expensive to install.
- **Dual Fuel / Hybrid:** Combines a heat pump with a gas furnace. The heat pump runs in mild weather, and the furnace kicks in during extreme cold.

### Components
- **Compressor:** The "heart" of the AC system. Pumps refrigerant between indoor and outdoor units.
- **Condenser:** The outdoor unit. Releases heat from refrigerant to the outside air.
- **Evaporator Coil:** The indoor coil. Absorbs heat from indoor air.
- **Air Handler:** The indoor unit that contains the blower and evaporator coil.
- **Blower Motor:** The motor that pushes air through the ductwork.
- **Capacitor:** An electrical component that helps start and run the compressor and fan motors. Common failure point.
- **Contactor:** An electrical switch in the outdoor unit. Opens and closes the circuit to the compressor.
- **Refrigerant:** The chemical that absorbs and releases heat. R-410A is current standard. R-22 (Freon) is obsolete and very expensive.
- **Expansion Valve (TXV):** Controls refrigerant flow into the evaporator coil.
- **Heat Exchanger:** The component inside a furnace that transfers heat from combustion gases to the air.
- **Ignitor:** Lights the burners in a gas furnace. Hot surface ignitors are most common.
- **Flame Sensor:** Detects whether the burner is lit. Dirty flame sensors are a top furnace repair call.
- **Draft Inducer Motor:** Pulls combustion gases through the heat exchanger and out the flue.
- **Plenum:** The box that connects the HVAC unit to the ductwork.

### Efficiency Ratings
- **SEER / SEER2:** Seasonal Energy Efficiency Ratio. Measures cooling efficiency. Higher is better. Minimum is now 14-15 SEER depending on region. High-efficiency units are 18-25+ SEER.
- **AFUE:** Annual Fuel Utilization Efficiency. Measures furnace efficiency. 80% AFUE means 80% of fuel becomes heat. High-efficiency is 90%-98%.
- **HSPF / HSPF2:** Heating Seasonal Performance Factor. Measures heat pump heating efficiency.
- **EER:** Energy Efficiency Ratio. Like SEER but measured at a single temperature point.
- **ENERGY STAR:** A government certification for equipment that meets high-efficiency standards.

### Common Brand Names Customers May Reference
- **Premium:** Carrier, Lennox, Trane
- **Mid-Range:** Rheem, Ruud, Bryant, American Standard, York
- **Value:** Goodman, Amana, Daikin (also makes premium), Payne
- **Ductless Specialists:** Mitsubishi, Fujitsu, LG, Daikin
- **Thermostats:** Nest (Google), Ecobee, Honeywell, Emerson Sensi

### Other Terms
- **Tonnage:** A measure of cooling capacity. 1 ton = 12,000 BTU/hr. Most homes need 2 to 5 tons.
- **BTU:** British Thermal Unit. A measure of heat energy.
- **Load Calculation (Manual J):** The proper way to size an HVAC system for a specific home.
- **Refrigerant Charge:** The amount of refrigerant in the system. Must be precise for proper operation.
- **Ductwork:** The network of metal or flexible tubes that distributes heated/cooled air.
- **Return Air:** The air pulled back into the system from the house to be re-conditioned.
- **Supply Air:** The conditioned air pushed out through vents into rooms.
- **Zoning:** Dividing a home into separate temperature zones, each with its own thermostat.
- **Variable Speed:** A blower or compressor that adjusts its speed to match demand. Quieter and more efficient.
- **Two-Stage:** A system with two output levels (low and high) instead of just on/off. Better comfort and efficiency.

## 5. Common Customer Questions and Ideal Responses

### Pricing Questions

**Q1: "How much does a new AC unit cost?"**
A: "Central AC installation typically ranges from about $3,800 to $8,000 depending on the size of your home, the efficiency rating you choose, and any ductwork modifications needed. High-efficiency systems can run $5,000 to $10,000 or more. I can schedule a technician to evaluate your home and give you an exact quote - there's no obligation."

**Q2: "How much does a furnace replacement cost?"**
A: "Gas furnace replacement typically runs between $2,500 and $7,500 depending on the size, efficiency level, and any ductwork or venting changes needed. Our technician can assess your current setup and give you an exact price. Would you like to schedule an estimate?"

**Q3: "How much is a service call?"**
A: "Our diagnostic fee is [company-specific - usually $75-$150]. This covers the technician coming out, diagnosing the issue, and providing a repair quote. In most cases, if you proceed with the repair, the diagnostic fee is applied toward the total cost."

**Q4: "Can you give me an exact price over the phone?"**
A: "I understand you'd like a specific number. Without seeing your system and the specific issue, I can give you a general range, but the exact cost depends on factors like your system type, what parts are needed, and accessibility. Our technician will give you a firm price before starting any work, so there are no surprises."

**Q5: "Your competitor quoted me less."**
A: "I appreciate you doing your research. Price is important, and I'd also encourage you to compare what's included - things like warranty coverage, the equipment brand, and whether the quote includes all labor and materials. We stand behind our work with [company warranty details]. Would you like to schedule a free estimate so you can compare apples to apples?"

### System Questions

**Q6: "How long does an HVAC system last?"**
A: "Most AC units and furnaces last 15 to 20 years with proper maintenance. Heat pumps typically last 12 to 15 years. If your system is over 10 years old and needing frequent repairs, it may be more cost-effective to replace it."

**Q7: "Should I repair or replace my system?"**
A: "A good rule of thumb is if the repair costs more than half the price of a new system, or if your system is over 12-15 years old and breaking down frequently, replacement is usually the better investment. Our technician can help you weigh both options."

**Q8: "What size system do I need?"**
A: "System sizing depends on your home's square footage, insulation, number of windows, layout, and climate zone. Our technician performs a load calculation to determine the right size. An undersized system will run constantly, and an oversized system will short-cycle and not dehumidify properly."

**Q9: "What brand do you recommend?"**
A: "We work with [company's brands]. All major brands today are reliable when properly installed. The most important factor is correct sizing and quality installation, which is what our team focuses on."

**Q10: "How often should I change my filter?"**
A: "For standard 1-inch filters, every 1 to 3 months depending on usage, pets, and allergies. For 4-inch or 5-inch media filters, every 6 to 12 months. A dirty filter is the number one cause of system breakdowns."

### Scheduling and Service Questions

**Q11: "How soon can someone come out?"**
A: "Let me check our schedule. [Check availability.] For emergencies like no heat in freezing temperatures or no cooling in extreme heat, we prioritize those calls."

**Q12: "Do you offer financing?"**
A: "Yes, we offer financing options for larger installations and replacements. Our technician can go over the details during your appointment, or I can have someone from our team call you to discuss options."

**Q13: "Do you offer warranties?"**
A: "All new equipment comes with a manufacturer warranty, typically 5 to 10 years on parts. We also provide a workmanship warranty on our installation and repairs. [Company-specific details.]"

**Q14: "Do I need to be home for the appointment?"**
A: "Yes, someone 18 or older needs to be present. The technician will need access to your indoor and outdoor equipment, and they'll want to walk you through their findings."

**Q15: "What maintenance should I be doing?"**
A: "The three most important things: change your filter regularly, keep the outdoor unit clear of debris and vegetation (2 feet of clearance), and schedule professional tune-ups twice a year, once for cooling and once for heating."

### Emergency/Problem Questions

**Q16: "My AC is blowing warm air."**
A: "That's a common issue. Before we schedule a visit, can you check a couple things? First, make sure your thermostat is set to 'cool' and the fan is set to 'auto.' Second, check if your outdoor unit is running. If those look fine, we should get a technician out to diagnose it."

**Q17: "My furnace won't turn on."**
A: "Let's troubleshoot a few things. Is your thermostat set to 'heat' and set above the current room temperature? Check that the furnace switch (usually looks like a light switch near the unit) is in the ON position. Also check your circuit breaker. If all that looks good, we'll send a tech out."

**Q18: "There's water leaking from my unit."**
A: "Water near your indoor unit usually means a clogged condensate drain line. Turn off your system at the thermostat to prevent further leaking, and we'll get a technician out to clear the drain. If the water is near your outdoor unit in summer, a small amount of condensation is normal."

**Q19: "My energy bills are unusually high."**
A: "Several things can cause that - a dirty filter, a refrigerant leak, a failing component making the system work harder, or duct leaks. A tune-up and system evaluation can identify the issue. When was the last time your system was serviced?"

**Q20: "I hear a strange noise from my HVAC."**
A: "Can you describe the noise? Banging could mean a loose part. Squealing often indicates a belt or motor issue. Clicking when the system starts is usually normal, but constant clicking may signal an electrical problem. Hissing could be a refrigerant leak. I'd recommend having a technician take a look."

### Handling Difficult Situations

**Angry customer about a previous repair:**
A: "I'm sorry to hear about that experience. Let me get your information so our service manager can review what happened and get this resolved for you. We want to make this right."

**Insurance claim question:**
A: "We can provide documentation of the damage and our repair estimate, which you can submit to your insurance company. The insurance process is between you and your provider, but we're happy to help with any paperwork they need."

## 6. Licensing and Compliance Notes

### Permits
- **New installations and replacements:** Most jurisdictions require a mechanical permit for HVAC installation or replacement. The contractor typically pulls this permit.
- **Ductwork modifications:** Often require a permit, especially if structural changes are involved.
- **Electrical work:** If the HVAC install requires new circuits or panel upgrades, a separate electrical permit may be needed.
- **Gas line work:** Requires a plumbing or mechanical permit in most areas.

### What to Tell Callers About Permits:
- "Our technician handles all necessary permits as part of the installation. The permit cost is included in your quote."
- If asked about doing their own install: "HVAC installation requires proper licensing, and most local codes require a licensed contractor to pull the permit. This also protects your equipment warranty."

### Common Code Requirements Homeowners Ask About:
- Minimum SEER ratings vary by region (14-15 SEER2 minimum as of 2023 federal standards)
- Furnaces must be properly vented (high-efficiency uses PVC, standard uses metal flue)
- Outdoor units typically need minimum clearances from property lines, windows, and gas meters
- Refrigerant handling requires EPA Section 608 certification
- Ductwork must meet code for insulation, material, and fire ratings

## 7. Upsell and Cross-Sell Awareness

### Natural Add-On Opportunities:

**During a Repair Call:**
- Maintenance plan: "Since we're already servicing your system, I should mention our maintenance plan. It includes two tune-ups per year and discounts on repairs. It helps prevent breakdowns like this one."
- Air filter subscription: "Would you like us to set up automatic filter delivery so you always have fresh filters on hand?"

**During a Replacement:**
- UV air purifier or air scrubber
- Whole-house humidifier or dehumidifier
- Zoning system for multi-level homes
- Extended warranty
- Maintenance plan

**How to Mention Without Being Pushy:**
- Frame it as information, not a sales pitch: "A lot of our customers also ask about..."

- Let the technician handle the in-person upsell. The phone agent's job is to book the appointment.

## 8. Red Flags and Liability Avoidance

### NEVER Say:
- "It's probably just a [specific diagnosis]." (You are not a technician. Do not diagnose.)
- "That will cost exactly $X." (Always give ranges and clarify that the tech provides exact pricing.)
- "Your system is definitely safe to run." (You cannot assess safety over the phone.)
- "You don't need a professional for that." (Liability issue.)
- "That should be covered under warranty." (Warranty coverage depends on specifics you may not have.)
- "We guarantee the lowest price." (Unless the company actually has a price-match guarantee.)
- "Your old system is fine, don't worry about it." (Dismissing potential safety concerns.)

### Phrases That Create Liability:
- "I promise..." (Use "we'll do our best to..." instead)
- "Guaranteed to fix..." (Use "our technician will diagnose and repair..." instead)
- "There's nothing wrong with..." (Use "our technician can take a look to be sure" instead)
- "You should try fixing it yourself by..." (Never give DIY repair advice for gas or electrical systems)

### Handling Threats:
**"I'm going to call the BBB / leave a bad review":**
A: "I understand your frustration, and I want to help resolve this. Let me connect you with our service manager who can address your concerns directly. We take customer satisfaction seriously."

**"I'm going to sue":**
A: "I'm sorry you feel that way. I'd like to try to resolve this for you. Let me have our manager reach out to discuss the situation. Can I get the best number to reach you?"

**"I'm calling my lawyer":**
A: "I understand. For any legal matters, please have your attorney contact us at [company's business address/phone]. In the meantime, I'd still like to try to resolve this - can I connect you with a manager?"

**General Rule:** Stay calm, empathetic, and professional. Do not argue, get defensive, or make admissions of fault. Escalate to a manager.
