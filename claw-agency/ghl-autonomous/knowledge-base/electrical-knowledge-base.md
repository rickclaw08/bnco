## 2. Emergency Protocols

### TRUE EMERGENCIES - Tell caller to call 911 FIRST:
- Active electrical fire or sparking/arcing with smoke
- Electrical shock injury (someone was shocked and is hurt)
- Downed power line on or near property
- Burning smell from electrical panel with visible damage
- Water in contact with live electrical equipment/wiring

### URGENT (Same-Day Service Needed):
- Total power loss in part of the home (not a utility outage)
- Burning or hot smell from outlets, switches, or panel (no visible fire)
- Outlet or switch is hot to the touch
- Sparking from outlet or switch when in use
- Breaker trips immediately and repeatedly after resetting
- Buzzing or humming from electrical panel
- Flickering lights throughout the house (not just one fixture)
- Exposed wires (from storm damage, renovation, or wear)

### ROUTINE (Schedule Within Normal Business Hours):
- Single outlet not working
- Light fixture not turning on
- Breaker trips occasionally
- Want to add outlets or circuits
- Ceiling fan installation
- EV charger installation
- Panel upgrade planning
- Lighting upgrade project
- Home inspection follow-up items

### Triage Questions to Ask:
1. "Is there any smoke, burning smell, or visible fire?" (If yes, tell them to call 911 and evacuate)
2. "Has anyone been shocked or injured?" (If yes, call 911)
3. "Is there a downed power line involved?" (If yes, stay far away and call 911)
4. "Is the issue in one area of your home, or is it affecting the whole house?"
5. "Have you checked your circuit breaker panel? Is anything tripped?"
6. "Is the problem in an area that's been exposed to water?"
7. "How old is your home?" (Helps identify wiring type)
8. "Have you had any recent electrical work or renovations?"
9. "Are your lights flickering, or is power completely out?"
10. "Is your neighbor's power also out?" (Helps determine if it's a utility issue)

### Safety Instructions for Callers:

**Burning Smell from Electrical:**
- "Turn off the breaker for that area if you can safely reach the panel."
- "If the smell is coming from the panel itself, turn off the main breaker."
- "Do not use the affected outlet, switch, or circuit until an electrician inspects it."
- "If you see smoke or flames, evacuate and call 911."

**Outlet or Switch Is Hot:**
- "Stop using that outlet or switch immediately."
- "Unplug anything connected to it."
- "If you can safely reach the breaker panel, turn off the breaker for that circuit."
- "Do not use it again until an electrician evaluates it."

**Breaker Keeps Tripping:**
- "Do not keep resetting the breaker repeatedly, as this can cause damage or a fire hazard."
- "Unplug everything on that circuit and try resetting once. If it trips again with nothing plugged in, leave it off and call us."

**Partial Power Loss:**
- "Check your main breaker panel for any tripped breakers."
- "If your stove and dryer work but lights are dim, you may have lost one phase of your electrical service. This is a utility issue - call your power company."

**Downed Power Line:**
- "Stay at least 35 feet away from the line. Do not touch it or anything it's touching."
- "Call 911 and your utility company."
- "If a line is on your car, stay in the car unless there's a fire."

- **Winter:** Test whole-house generator monthly, check that space heaters are on dedicated circuits, inspect holiday light connections

## 4. Industry Terminology Glossary

### Electrical Basics
- **Amperage (Amps):** The amount of electrical current flowing through a circuit. Like water flow rate.
- **Voltage (Volts):** The electrical pressure pushing current through wires. Standard US outlets are 120V. Dryers, ranges, and EV chargers use 240V.
- **Wattage (Watts):** The rate of energy use. Amps x Volts = Watts.
- **Circuit:** A complete electrical path from the panel through wiring to outlets/fixtures and back.
- **Breaker:** A safety switch in the electrical panel that cuts power when a circuit draws too much current.
- **Fuse:** Older version of a breaker. A wire that melts to break the circuit. Found in pre-1960s homes. Should be upgraded.
- **Ground/Grounding:** A safety path for stray electrical current to travel to the earth, preventing shocks.
- **Short Circuit:** When electricity takes an unintended path, often causing a breaker to trip instantly.
- **Arc Fault:** An unintended electrical discharge that can cause fires. AFCI breakers detect these.
- **Ground Fault:** When electricity leaks from the intended path to a grounded surface. GFCI devices detect these.

### Panel and Distribution
- **Electrical Panel (Breaker Box, Load Center):** The main distribution point for electricity in a home. Contains breakers.
- **Main Breaker:** The large breaker at the top of the panel that controls all power to the home.
- **Service Entrance:** Where utility power enters the home (meter, weatherhead, main disconnect).
- **200-Amp Service:** Standard modern home electrical service. Older homes may have 100-amp or even 60-amp.
- **Subpanel:** A secondary breaker panel that distributes power to a specific area (garage, addition, workshop).
- **Bus Bar:** The metal bars inside the panel that distribute power to individual breakers.
- **Double-Tap:** Two wires connected to a single breaker terminal. A code violation and fire hazard.
- **Tandem Breaker:** Two thin breakers in one slot. Allowed in some panels, not others.
- **Federal Pacific / Zinsco Panels:** Obsolete panels known for safety hazards. Should be replaced immediately.

### Wiring Types
- **Romex (NM Cable):** The most common residential wiring. Plastic-sheathed cable with copper conductors.
- **Knob-and-Tube:** Oldest residential wiring type (pre-1950s). Ungrounded, no longer meets code, fire risk.
- **Aluminum Wiring:** Used in 1960s-1970s. Connection points are fire hazards. Requires remediation.
- **BX/MC Cable:** Metal-sheathed cable. Common in older urban homes and commercial buildings.
- **THHN/THWN:** Individual conductor wire run inside conduit.
- **Wire Gauge:** Wire thickness. Lower number = thicker wire = more capacity. 14 AWG for 15-amp circuits, 12 AWG for 20-amp, 10 AWG for 30-amp.

### Devices and Components
- **GFCI (Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter):** Outlet with test/reset buttons. Required in bathrooms, kitchens, garages, outdoors, and near water. Cuts power in milliseconds if it detects a ground fault.
- **AFCI (Arc Fault Circuit Interrupter):** Breaker that detects dangerous arc faults. Required in bedrooms and most living areas by modern code.
- **Dedicated Circuit:** A circuit serving only one appliance (required for refrigerators, dishwashers, microwaves, washing machines, etc.).
- **Transfer Switch:** Switches between utility power and generator power. Required for all generator installations.
- **Surge Protector (Whole-House):** Installed at the panel to protect the entire home from power surges.
- **Junction Box:** An enclosed box where wire connections are made. All connections must be inside a box per code.
- **Conduit:** Metal or plastic tubing that protects wires. Required in exposed locations.

### EV Charging
- **Level 1:** Standard 120V outlet. Very slow (3-5 miles of range per hour). No special installation needed.
- **Level 2:** 240V circuit. The standard home charger (25-30 miles of range per hour). Requires a dedicated 40-50 amp circuit.
- **NEMA 14-50:** The most common outlet type for EV chargers. Same as a range outlet.
- **Hardwired vs Plug-In:** Chargers can be permanently wired or plug into an outlet. Both work; plug-in is more portable.

### Common Brand Names
- **Panels:** Square D (Schneider Electric), Eaton (Cutler-Hammer), Siemens, GE, Leviton
- **Outlets/Switches:** Leviton, Lutron (dimmers and smart switches), Legrand, Hubbell
- **Generators:** Generac, Cummins, Kohler, Briggs and Stratton, Champion
- **EV Chargers:** ChargePoint, Tesla Wall Connector, JuiceBox, Emporia, Wallbox, Grizzl-E
- **Smart Home:** Lutron Caseta, Leviton Decora Smart, TP-Link Kasa, Wemo
- **Problematic Panels (Suggest Replacement):** Federal Pacific (Stab-Lok), Zinsco/Sylvania, Pushmatic

## 5. Common Customer Questions and Ideal Responses

### Pricing Questions

**Q1: "How much does it cost to add an outlet?"**
A: "A new standard outlet installation typically runs $125 to $350 depending on how far it is from the panel and the wiring route. GFCI outlets and 240V outlets cost a bit more. Our electrician will give you an exact quote."

**Q2: "How much is a panel upgrade?"**
A: "Upgrading from 100-amp to 200-amp service typically costs $1,000 to $4,000 depending on the existing setup and any utility coordination needed. It's one of the best investments you can make in an older home."

**Q3: "How much to install an EV charger?"**
A: "Level 2 EV charger installation typically runs $500 to $2,500 depending on how far your panel is from the garage, whether your panel has capacity, and the charger model. Our electrician can assess your setup and give you a firm price."

**Q4: "How much to rewire my house?"**
A: "Whole-house rewiring typically costs $3 to $5 per square foot, so for a 1,500-square-foot home, you're looking at roughly $4,500 to $7,500. Larger or older homes with more complex layouts cost more. An electrician needs to evaluate your home for an accurate quote."

### Problem Questions

**Q5: "My breaker keeps tripping."**
A: "That's a safety mechanism doing its job, but it means something is overloading or shorting on that circuit. A breaker that trips regularly should be evaluated. Don't keep resetting it without finding the cause. Is this a new issue, or has it been happening for a while?"

**Q6: "My lights flicker."**
A: "Occasional flickering in one fixture is usually a loose bulb or connection. If lights throughout your home flicker, especially when large appliances kick on, it could indicate a panel issue, loose main connection, or an undersized electrical service. How widespread is the flickering?"

**Q7: "I have a two-prong outlet. Can I just replace it with a three-prong?"**
A: "You can't simply swap it because a three-prong outlet requires a ground wire that likely isn't there. An electrician can evaluate your options: running a new grounded circuit, installing a GFCI outlet (which is code-compliant without a ground), or rewiring that circuit."

**Q8: "Is aluminum wiring dangerous?"**
A: "Aluminum wiring itself conducts electricity fine, but the connections are the concern. Aluminum expands and contracts differently than copper, which can loosen connections and create fire hazards. An electrician can inspect your connections and either pigtail them with copper or recommend other remediation."

**Q9: "My electrical panel is a Federal Pacific / Zinsco. Should I be worried?"**
A: "Those panels have a documented history of breakers failing to trip during overloads, which is a serious fire risk. Most electricians and home inspectors recommend replacing them. I'd suggest scheduling an evaluation so our electrician can assess your specific panel and give you options."

**Q10: "Do I need a permit to add an outlet?"**
A: "In most areas, yes, electrical work requires a permit. Our electrician handles the permit and any required inspections as part of the job."

**Q11: "Can I do my own electrical work?"**
A: "Homeowners can do some basic electrical work in many states, but it still needs to meet code and may require a permit and inspection. For safety and insurance reasons, we recommend having a licensed electrician handle it, especially anything involving the panel, new circuits, or 240V work."

**Q12: "My smoke detector keeps beeping."**
A: "A single chirp every 30-60 seconds usually means the battery needs replacing, even on hardwired units - they have backup batteries. If it's a continuous alarm, check for actual smoke or CO. If you need a hardwired replacement or have units that are over 10 years old, we can replace them."

### Scheduling and Project Questions

**Q13: "How long does a panel upgrade take?"**
A: "Most panel upgrades take about 4 to 8 hours. Your power will be off for most of that time. The utility company may need to disconnect and reconnect your service, which our electrician coordinates."

**Q14: "How long does rewiring take?"**
A: "For a full rewire, expect 3 to 7 days for an average home. There will be some drywall repair needed afterward - some electricians include patching, others recommend a drywall contractor."

**Q15: "Should I upgrade my panel before buying an EV?"**
A: "Not necessarily. Many homes with 200-amp service have enough capacity for a Level 2 charger without an upgrade. Our electrician can evaluate your panel and load to determine if an upgrade is needed."

**Q16: "My home inspector found issues. Can you fix them?"**
A: "Absolutely. Bring the inspection report to the appointment or email it to us in advance. Our electrician can address each item and provide documentation for the sale."

**Q17: "Do you install smart home systems?"**
A: "We handle the electrical side - smart switches, smart outlets, and the wiring infrastructure. For full smart home integration with voice assistants and hub setup, we can recommend partners."

**Q18: "What's the difference between 15-amp and 20-amp outlets?"**
A: "A 20-amp circuit carries more power and is required in kitchens, bathrooms, laundry rooms, and garages. The outlet has a small T-shaped slot on one side. General rooms typically use 15-amp circuits."

**Q19: "Why do my outlets have only two prongs?"**
A: "Two-prong outlets mean ungrounded wiring, which is common in homes built before the 1960s. They still work but don't have the safety protection of a ground. We can upgrade them to grounded three-prong outlets."

**Q20: "Can I add more circuits to my current panel?"**
A: "It depends on whether your panel has open spaces (breaker slots) and whether the panel's total amperage can support additional load. Our electrician can inspect your panel and let you know your options."

### Handling Difficult Situations

**Price shoppers:**
A: "Electrical work directly affects your family's safety. Our electricians are fully licensed, insured, and pull proper permits. We're happy to provide a detailed quote so you can compare what's included."

**DIY gone wrong:**
A: "No judgment. Let's get it evaluated and fixed safely. Our electrician can inspect what's been done and bring it up to code."

## 6. Licensing and Compliance Notes

### Permits
- **Almost all electrical work requires a permit** in most jurisdictions, including new circuits, panel changes, rewiring, and fixture installations.
- **Exceptions (in some areas):** Like-for-like replacements (swapping an outlet or switch) may not need a permit, but check local codes.
- **Inspections:** Most permitted work requires inspection before being covered up (walls closed).
- **The electrician handles permits** unless the homeowner is doing their own work.

### What to Tell Callers:
- "Our electrician pulls all required permits and schedules inspections as part of the job."
- "Permits protect you by ensuring the work meets code and is inspected by the city."
- "Unpermitted electrical work can create problems when selling your home and may void insurance coverage."

### Common Code Requirements:
- GFCI protection required in kitchens (within 6 feet of sink), bathrooms, garages, outdoors, basements, crawl spaces, laundry areas
- AFCI protection required in bedrooms, living rooms, dining rooms, hallways, closets (varies by code version)
- Smoke detectors required in each bedroom, outside each sleeping area, and on every level
- CO detectors required near sleeping areas in homes with gas appliances or attached garages
- Tamper-resistant outlets required in all new or replacement installations
- All outlets within 6 feet of water must be GFCI protected
- Outdoor outlets must have weatherproof (in-use) covers
- All wire connections must be inside approved junction boxes

## 8. Red Flags and Liability Avoidance

### NEVER Say:
- "Your wiring is probably fine." (You cannot assess electrical safety over the phone.)
- "Just reset the breaker and it should be okay." (Could mask a serious problem.)
- "That's an easy fix, you can do it yourself." (Liability - electrical work can kill.)
- "It definitely costs $X." (Always ranges.)
- "Your panel is safe." (Cannot determine remotely.)
- "You don't need a permit for that." (Almost all electrical work requires a permit.)
- "Federal Pacific panels are fine, lots of people still have them." (Known fire hazard.)
- "Just use an adapter for that two-prong outlet." (Not a code-compliant solution.)

### Phrases That Create Liability:
- "I guarantee..." (Use "our electrician will evaluate and provide options")
- "It's safe to..." (Use "our electrician can determine if it's safe")
- "You won't have any more problems." (Use "this repair should resolve the issue")
- "Your insurance will cover that." (You don't know their policy)

### Handling Threats:
**BBB/Review threats:**
A: "I'm sorry you're unhappy. Let me connect you with our service manager to resolve this. We value your feedback and want to make it right."

**Legal threats:**
A: "I understand your frustration. For legal matters, please contact us at [company address]. I'd like to try to resolve this first - may I have a manager call you?"

**General Rule:** Do not provide electrical advice over the phone beyond basic safety instructions (evacuate, turn off breaker, don't touch). Electrical issues can be lethal. Always defer to the licensed electrician for any technical guidance.
