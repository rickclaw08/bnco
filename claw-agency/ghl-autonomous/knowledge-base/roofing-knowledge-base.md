## 2. Emergency Protocols

### TRUE EMERGENCIES - Tell caller to call 911 FIRST, then call us back:
- Roof collapse or partial structural failure (anyone trapped or in danger)
- Tree fell through the roof and people are inside
- Exposed electrical wiring from storm damage
- Fire damage to the roof structure

### URGENT (Same-Day/Next-Day Service Needed):
- Active leak with water pouring into the home during a storm
- Large section of roof missing after severe weather (home exposed to elements)
- Tree or large branch resting on the roof but no collapse yet
- Tarped roof where the tarp has blown off and rain is expected
- Visible sagging or bowing of the roofline (structural failure risk)
- Ceiling drywall bulging from trapped water (risk of ceiling collapse)
- Hail damage with active leaking

### ROUTINE (Schedule Within Normal Business Hours):
- Missing a few shingles but no active leak
- Minor leak that only appears during heavy rain
- Granule loss on shingles (end-of-life wear)
- Gutter damage or detachment
- Flashing pulling away from chimney or walls
- Moss or algae growth on shingles
- Wanting a roof inspection or estimate
- Cosmetic damage (discoloration, staining)
- Preparing for insurance claim documentation

### Triage Questions to Ask:
1. "Is anyone injured or trapped?" (If yes, tell them to call 911 immediately)
2. "Is water actively coming into your home right now?"
3. "Can you see sky through your roof, or is a large section missing?"
4. "Was there a recent storm, hail, or high-wind event?"
5. "Is there a tree or large branch on or against your roof?"
6. "Do you see any sagging, bowing, or drooping in your roofline or ceiling?"
7. "Is this an insurance claim or are you looking for a direct estimate?"
8. "How old is your current roof?"
9. "What type of roofing material do you have?" (shingles, tile, metal, flat)
10. "Is this a single-story or multi-story home?"

### Safety Instructions for Callers:
- Active leak: "Place buckets or containers under the leak to catch water. If the ceiling is bulging, stay out of that room, as the drywall could collapse under the weight of trapped water. Do not go on the roof yourself."
- Storm damage: "Stay inside and away from damaged areas. Do not attempt to climb on the roof, especially if it is wet or there are downed power lines nearby. We can send someone to assess the damage safely."
- Tree on roof: "Do not go under or near the damaged area. If you hear cracking or see the roof shifting, move everyone to the other side of the house or leave entirely."
- Missing shingles/tarp blew off: "If you can safely access the attic, place a bucket under any drip points. Do not attempt to re-tarp a roof yourself, as it is extremely dangerous in wind or on wet surfaces."
- Sagging roof: "Evacuate the room directly under the sag. A sagging roof can indicate structural failure, and weight from water, snow, or debris could cause a collapse."

## 4. Industry Terminology Glossary

### Roof Structure
- **Decking (Sheathing):** The plywood or OSB boards nailed to the rafters that form the base layer of the roof. Everything else sits on top of this.
- **Rafters:** The sloped framing members that support the roof decking. Found in older/stick-built roofs.
- **Trusses:** Pre-fabricated triangular framing units that support the roof. Common in modern construction.
- **Ridge:** The highest horizontal line of the roof where two sloping sides meet.
- **Ridge Board/Beam:** The horizontal board at the peak where rafters connect.
- **Eave:** The lower edge of the roof that overhangs the exterior wall.
- **Soffit:** The underside of the eave overhang. Usually vented for attic airflow.
- **Fascia:** The vertical board running along the eave edge, where gutters are attached.
- **Rake:** The sloped edge of a gable roof (the diagonal side).
- **Valley:** The internal angle where two roof slopes meet. High-traffic area for water flow.
- **Hip:** The external angle where two roof slopes meet (opposite of a valley).
- **Dormer:** A structure that projects out from a sloped roof, usually containing a window.
- **Gable:** The triangular wall section between the edges of intersecting roof pitches.
- **Pitch (Slope):** The steepness of the roof, expressed as rise-over-run (e.g., 6/12 means 6 inches of rise per 12 inches of horizontal run).
- **Square:** A roofing measurement equal to 100 square feet of roof area.
- **Roof Deck:** Another term for sheathing/decking.

### Roofing Materials
- **3-Tab Shingles:** The most basic asphalt shingle. Flat appearance, 15-20 year lifespan. Budget option.
- **Architectural Shingles (Dimensional/Laminate):** Multi-layered asphalt shingle with a thicker, textured appearance. 25-30 year lifespan. Most popular residential option.
- **Impact-Resistant Shingles (Class 4):** Shingles rated to withstand hail impact. Often qualify for insurance discounts.
- **Standing Seam Metal:** Metal panels with raised seams that interlock. Premium durability and wind resistance.
- **Corrugated Metal:** Wavy or ribbed metal panels. Lower cost than standing seam.
- **Clay Tile:** Fired clay tiles, common in Mediterranean and Spanish-style architecture. Very heavy, 50-100+ year life.
- **Concrete Tile:** Similar look to clay but made of concrete. Slightly less expensive, also very heavy.
- **Slate:** Natural stone roofing. The longest-lasting material (75-200 years). Extremely heavy and expensive.
- **Wood Shake:** Split or sawn wood (typically cedar). Natural look, 20-40 year lifespan. Fire risk in some regions.
- **Wood Shingle:** Thinner and more uniform than shakes. Machine-cut.
- **TPO (Thermoplastic Polyolefin):** A white single-ply membrane for flat/low-slope roofs. Energy-efficient.
- **EPDM:** Ethylene propylene diene monomer. A black rubber membrane for flat roofs. Durable and affordable.
- **PVC Membrane:** Similar to TPO. Chemical-resistant, used on flat/commercial roofs.
- **Built-Up Roof (BUR):** Multiple layers of asphalt and felt/fiberglass. Traditional flat roof system ("tar and gravel").
- **Modified Bitumen:** Asphalt-based rolled roofing for flat or low-slope roofs. Applied with heat or adhesive.
- **Synthetic/Composite:** Engineered materials that mimic slate, wood, or tile. Lighter and less expensive than natural counterparts.

### Underlayment and Barriers
- **Underlayment:** A water-resistant layer installed on the decking before the roofing material. Felt (tar paper) or synthetic.
- **Ice and Water Shield:** A self-adhesive waterproof membrane installed in vulnerable areas (eaves, valleys, around penetrations). Required in cold climates.
- **Drip Edge:** A metal strip along the eaves and rakes that directs water away from the fascia and into the gutter.
- **Starter Strip:** A row of shingles (or shingle material) installed at the eave edge before the first course. Provides a seal against wind uplift.
- **Flashing:** Thin metal (aluminum, galvanized steel, copper) installed at joints and penetrations (chimneys, vents, skylights, walls) to prevent water entry.
- **Step Flashing:** L-shaped metal pieces woven into shingles where the roof meets a vertical wall.
- **Counter Flashing:** Metal embedded in masonry (chimney) that overlaps step flashing.
- **Valley Flashing:** Metal or membrane lining in roof valleys to channel water.

### Ventilation
- **Ridge Vent:** A vent running along the roof ridge. Allows hot air to escape from the attic.
- **Soffit Vent:** Vents in the soffit that allow cool air to enter the attic from below.
- **Gable Vent:** A vent in the gable wall for attic airflow.
- **Turbine Vent (Whirlybird):** A spinning vent on the roof that pulls hot air out of the attic.
- **Power Vent (Attic Fan):** An electric or solar-powered fan that exhausts hot air from the attic.
- **Balanced Ventilation:** The principle that intake (soffit) and exhaust (ridge) vents should be roughly equal for optimal airflow.

### Gutters and Drainage
- **Seamless Gutters:** Custom-formed gutters made from a single piece of aluminum. No seams means fewer leaks.
- **Sectional Gutters:** Pre-cut gutter pieces joined together. More leak-prone at seams.
- **Downspout:** The vertical pipe that carries water from the gutter to the ground.
- **Gutter Guard/Screen:** A cover or filter over the gutter that prevents leaves and debris from entering.
- **Splash Block:** A concrete or plastic block at the base of a downspout that directs water away from the foundation.
- **Ice Dam:** A ridge of ice at the eave that prevents melting snow from draining. Causes water to back up under shingles and leak inside.

### Other Terms
- **Roof-Over (Overlay):** Installing new shingles directly over the existing layer without tearing off. Allowed in some jurisdictions for one layer only.
- **Tear-Off:** Removing the existing roofing material down to the decking before installing new material.
- **Penetration:** Any object that passes through the roof surface (vents, pipes, skylights, chimneys).
- **Curb Appeal:** The visual attractiveness of a home from the street. A new roof significantly improves curb appeal.
- **Wind Uplift:** The force of wind pulling roofing materials away from the deck. Proper fastening and sealing prevent this.
- **Algae Resistance (AR Shingles):** Shingles treated with copper granules to prevent black algae streaks.
- **Cool Roof:** A roof designed to reflect more sunlight and absorb less heat. White membranes, reflective coatings, or light-colored materials.
- **R-Value:** A measure of insulation's thermal resistance. Higher R-value means better insulation.

### Common Brand Names Customers May Reference
- **Shingle Manufacturers:** GAF, Owens Corning, CertainTeed, IKO, Atlas, Tamko, PABCO
- **Metal Roofing:** Drexel Metals, McElroy Metal, Sheffield Metals, Fabral, Berridge
- **Tile:** Boral, Eagle Roofing Products, Ludowici, US Tile
- **Flat/Commercial:** Firestone, Carlisle, Johns Manville, Versico, Sika Sarnafil
- **Gutters:** LeafGuard, LeafFilter, Gutter Helmet (gutter guard brands)

## 5. Common Customer Questions and Ideal Responses

### Pricing Questions

**Q1: "How much does a new roof cost?"**
A: "A full roof replacement typically ranges from about $5,800 to $13,000 for asphalt shingles, with the national average around $9,500. The final cost depends on your roof's size, pitch, the material you choose, and whether the old roof needs to be torn off. Metal, tile, and slate roofs cost significantly more. I can schedule a free inspection and estimate so we can give you an exact number."

**Q2: "How much does a roof repair cost?"**
A: "Most roof repairs fall between $400 and $2,000, with the average around $1,150. Simple fixes like replacing a few shingles can be as low as $150, while structural repairs can reach $5,000 or more. Our technician will inspect the damage and give you a firm price before starting any work."

**Q3: "Can you give me a quote over the phone?"**
A: "I can give you a general range, but an accurate quote requires an in-person inspection. The cost depends on your roof's size, pitch, material, and the extent of damage or wear. Our estimator will come out, inspect everything, and provide a detailed written quote with no obligation."

**Q4: "Is it cheaper to repair or replace?"**
A: "If the damage is isolated to a small area, repair is almost always cheaper. But if your roof is older than 20 years, has widespread damage, or you are making frequent repair calls, a full replacement is usually the smarter long-term investment. Our estimator can walk you through both options and help you decide."

**Q5: "Does insurance cover this?"**
A: "Homeowner's insurance typically covers sudden, accidental damage from storms, hail, fallen trees, and similar events. It usually does not cover normal wear and tear or lack of maintenance. We can inspect the damage, provide documentation, and work directly with your insurance adjuster if needed."

### System and Material Questions

**Q6: "How long does a roof last?"**
A: "It depends on the material. Three-tab asphalt shingles last 15 to 20 years. Architectural shingles last 25 to 30 years. Metal roofs last 40 to 70 years. Tile can last 50 to 100 years, and slate can last 75 years or longer. Proper ventilation and maintenance significantly extend any roof's lifespan."

**Q7: "What is the best roofing material?"**
A: "There is no single best material - it depends on your budget, climate, and aesthetic preference. Architectural asphalt shingles are the most popular because they offer a great balance of cost, durability, and appearance. Metal is excellent for longevity and energy efficiency. Tile and slate are premium options that last the longest. Our estimator can recommend the best fit for your home."

**Q8: "Should I get a metal roof or shingles?"**
A: "Metal roofs cost more upfront but last 2 to 3 times longer than asphalt shingles, require less maintenance, and are more energy-efficient. Asphalt shingles are more affordable upfront and come in a wide variety of styles. If you plan to stay in your home long-term, metal often pays for itself. We can show you options for both."

**Q9: "What are architectural shingles?"**
A: "Architectural shingles, also called dimensional or laminate shingles, are a premium asphalt shingle with multiple layers that create a thicker, more textured appearance. They last 25 to 30 years compared to 15 to 20 for basic 3-tab shingles, and they offer better wind resistance. Most homeowners choose architectural shingles for replacements today."

**Q10: "Can you put new shingles over old ones?"**
A: "In some cases, yes. It is called a roof-over or overlay. Most building codes allow one layer of shingles over an existing layer. However, a tear-off is usually recommended because it lets us inspect the decking for rot or damage, results in a cleaner installation, and does not add extra weight to the structure. We can advise you after seeing your roof."

## 6. Licensing and Compliance Notes

### Permits
- **Roof replacements:** Most jurisdictions require a building permit for a full roof replacement. The contractor typically pulls this permit.
- **Structural repairs:** Truss, rafter, or decking repairs often require a separate structural permit or engineering approval.
- **Inspections:** Many municipalities require a final inspection after a roof replacement to confirm code compliance.

### What to Tell Callers About Permits:
- "We handle all permitting as part of your project. The cost is included in your estimate."
- If asked about DIY: "Roofing work requires proper licensing in most areas, and building codes require professional installation for permits to be issued. Doing it yourself could also void your material warranty."

### Common Code Requirements Homeowners Ask About:
- Minimum wind resistance ratings vary by region (higher in coastal and hurricane-prone zones)
- Ice and water shield required in cold climates within a certain distance of the eave
- Maximum of two layers of asphalt shingles in most jurisdictions (many require tear-off to one layer)
- Proper ventilation requirements (1 sq ft of ventilation per 150 sq ft of attic floor, or 1:300 with balanced intake/exhaust)
- Flashing requirements at all penetrations and wall intersections
- Drip edge required at eaves and rakes in most areas
- Specific fastener patterns and counts in high-wind zones (e.g., 6-nail pattern vs 4-nail)
- Impact-resistant shingle requirements in hail-prone areas (may qualify for insurance discounts)

### Contractor Licensing Notes:
- Roofing contractors must be licensed in most states. Requirements vary by state.
- General liability insurance and workers' compensation coverage are standard requirements.
- Some states require specific roofing endorsements separate from a general contractor license.
- Always verify: license number, insurance certificates, bonding (if required by state).
- Manufacturer certifications (GAF Master Elite, Owens Corning Platinum Preferred, CertainTeed SELECT) indicate advanced training and allow the contractor to offer extended warranties.

## 8. Red Flags and Liability Avoidance

### NEVER Say:
- "Your roof is fine." (You cannot assess a roof over the phone.)
- "It will definitely cost $X." (Always give ranges and clarify that the estimator provides exact pricing.)
- "That is definitely covered by insurance." (Insurance coverage depends on the policy and adjuster's determination.)
- "You can probably just patch it yourself." (Liability issue, and roofs are dangerous for untrained people.)
- "Your roof should last another X years." (You cannot determine remaining lifespan without an inspection.)
- "We guarantee the lowest price." (Unless the company has a documented price-match policy.)
- "The other company is overcharging you." (Never disparage competitors.)
- "You do not need a permit for that." (Permit requirements vary and this can create legal issues.)

### Phrases That Create Liability:
- "I promise..." (Use "we will do our best to..." instead)
- "Guaranteed to stop the leak..." (Use "our technician will identify and repair the source of the leak" instead)
- "There is nothing wrong with your roof." (Use "our inspector can take a thorough look" instead)
- "You should climb up and check it yourself." (NEVER advise a homeowner to get on their roof)
- "That is just cosmetic, do not worry about it." (Use "our inspector can evaluate whether it needs attention" instead)

### Storm Chaser Red Flags (Educate callers when relevant):
- Contractors going door-to-door after storms with no local office
- Offering to pay insurance deductibles (this is insurance fraud)
- Requesting large upfront deposits before work begins
- Pressuring homeowners to sign contracts immediately
- No verifiable license number, insurance, or references
- Using a P.O. box instead of a physical business address

### Handling Threats:
**"I am going to call the BBB / leave a bad review":**
A: "I understand your frustration, and I want to help resolve this. Let me connect you with our project manager who can address your concerns directly. We take customer satisfaction seriously."

**"I am going to sue / I am calling my lawyer":**
A: "I am sorry you feel that way. I would like to try to resolve this for you. Let me have our manager reach out to discuss the situation. For any legal matters, please have your attorney contact us at [company address/phone]."

**General Rule:** Stay calm, empathetic, and professional. Do not argue, get defensive, or make admissions of fault. Escalate to a manager.
