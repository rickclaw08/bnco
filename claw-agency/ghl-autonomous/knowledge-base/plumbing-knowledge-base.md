## 2. Emergency Protocols

### TRUE EMERGENCIES - Tell caller to call 911 FIRST, then call us back:
- Gas leak (rotten egg smell) - Evacuate the home immediately, do not use any switches or phones inside
- Sewage backing up into living spaces creating health hazard with no way to stop flow
- Water flooding a home with electrical outlets/appliances submerged (electrocution risk)

### URGENT (Same-Day/Emergency Service Needed):
- Major water leak or burst pipe - active flooding
- No hot water in freezing temperatures (risk of pipes freezing)
- Sewer backup into home (all drains backing up)
- Frozen pipes (before they burst)
- Gas line issues (smell without active leak, pilot light problems)
- Water heater leaking significantly
- Overflowing toilet that cannot be stopped with shut-off valve
- Main water line break

### ROUTINE (Schedule Within Normal Business Hours):
- Slow drains
- Dripping faucets
- Running toilet
- Low water pressure
- Minor leaks under sinks
- Water heater not producing enough hot water
- Garbage disposal jammed
- Outdoor faucet dripping

### Triage Questions to Ask:
1. "Do you smell gas or rotten eggs?" (If yes, tell them to evacuate and call 911)
2. "Is there active flooding or water damage happening right now?"
3. "Can you locate the water shut-off valve and turn it off?" (Walk them through it if needed)
4. "Is the issue affecting one fixture or all fixtures in your home?"
5. "Is sewage coming up through your drains?"
6. "Are there any elderly people, children, or anyone with medical needs in the home?"
7. "What is the temperature outside?" (Important for frozen pipe risk)
8. "When did you first notice the problem?"
9. "Is the water heater gas or electric?"
10. "Do you have a basement? Is it flooding?"

### Safety Instructions for Callers:

**Water Leak/Burst Pipe:**
- "First, let's stop the water. Your main shut-off valve is usually near where the water line enters your home, often in the basement, crawl space, or near the water meter. Turn it clockwise to shut it off."
- "If you can't find the shut-off, look for the valve near your water meter outside and turn that off."
- "Turn off your water heater to prevent damage to it. For gas water heaters, turn the gas valve to 'pilot' or 'off.' For electric, flip the breaker."
- "If water is near electrical outlets or your electrical panel, do not step in the water. Turn off power at the main breaker if you can safely reach it."

**Frozen Pipes:**
- "If the pipes haven't burst yet, open the faucet slightly so water can drip through. This relieves pressure."
- "You can apply gentle heat with a hair dryer or heat lamp. Never use an open flame or torch."
- "Open cabinet doors under sinks on exterior walls to let warm air circulate."

**Sewer Backup:**
- "Stop using all water in the home - do not flush toilets, run faucets, or use the washing machine."
- "If sewage is in a finished area, avoid contact and keep children and pets away."
- "Do not try to clean up sewage yourself if it's a large amount - it's a health hazard."

**Gas Smell:**
- "Leave the house immediately. Do not flip any light switches, use your phone inside, or start your car in the garage. Call 911 from outside, then call us."

### Seasonal Patterns:
- **Thanksgiving/Christmas week:** Drain clogs spike dramatically (cooking grease, food waste, extra guests using plumbing)
- **First hard freeze:** Burst pipe calls flood in. Response times extend significantly.
- **Spring thaw:** Sewer backups from ground shifting, root intrusion becomes apparent
- **Summer:** Best time for planned repiping, water heater replacement, and non-urgent upgrades

### Maintenance Recommendations by Season:
- **Spring:** Test sump pump, inspect outdoor faucets, check for winter damage, clean gutters (prevents foundation water issues)
- **Summer:** Check water heater, inspect washing machine hoses, address slow drains before they become clogs
- **Fall:** Disconnect outdoor hoses, insulate exposed pipes, schedule water heater flush, winterize outdoor faucets
- **Winter:** Keep heat at 55F or above even when away, open cabinets under exterior-wall sinks, drip faucets during extreme cold

## 4. Industry Terminology Glossary

### Pipe Types
- **Copper:** Traditional metal pipe. Durable, long-lasting (50+ years). Used for water supply lines. More expensive.
- **PEX (Cross-linked Polyethylene):** Flexible plastic pipe. Now the most common for new installs. Cheaper, easier to install, freeze-resistant.
- **PVC:** White plastic pipe. Used for drain, waste, and vent lines. Not for hot water supply.
- **CPVC:** Cream/yellow plastic pipe. Can handle hot water unlike regular PVC.
- **ABS:** Black plastic pipe. Used for drains and vents. Common in older homes.
- **Cast Iron:** Heavy metal drain pipe. Found in older homes. Lasts 75-100 years but eventually corrodes.
- **Galvanized Steel:** Old water supply pipe. Corrodes from inside, reduces flow over time. Should be replaced if found.
- **Polybutylene (Poly-B):** Gray plastic pipe used in 1978-1995. Prone to failure. Should be replaced.

### Fixtures and Components
- **Ball Valve:** Quarter-turn shut-off valve (lever handle). Most reliable type.
- **Gate Valve:** Round-handle shut-off valve. Older type, can seize up if not used regularly.
- **PRV (Pressure Reducing Valve):** Reduces incoming water pressure to safe levels (typically 40-80 PSI).
- **Expansion Tank:** A small tank on the water heater that absorbs pressure from thermal expansion.
- **P-Trap:** The U-shaped pipe under sinks. Holds water to block sewer gas from entering the home.
- **Wax Ring:** The seal between a toilet and the floor flange. Needs replacement if the toilet leaks at the base.
- **Flapper:** The rubber valve inside the toilet tank. A worn flapper causes running toilets.
- **Fill Valve:** The mechanism in the toilet tank that refills it after flushing.
- **Flange:** The connection point on the floor where the toilet sits. Also called a closet flange.
- **Aerator:** The screen on the end of a faucet. Mixes air with water to reduce splashing and save water.
- **Cartridge:** The internal valve mechanism in single-handle faucets.
- **Shut-Off Valve:** Individual valves under sinks and behind toilets to turn water off to that fixture.
- **Check Valve:** A one-way valve that prevents backflow.
- **Backflow Preventer:** Required device that prevents contaminated water from flowing back into the clean water supply.
- **Anode Rod:** A sacrificial rod inside water heaters that attracts corrosion to protect the tank. Should be replaced every 3-5 years.

### Water Heater Terms
- **Tank Water Heater:** Traditional style with a storage tank (40, 50, 75 gallon common sizes).
- **Tankless (On-Demand):** Heats water as it flows through. Endless hot water but limited flow rate.
- **First-Hour Rating:** How much hot water a tank heater can deliver in the first hour of use.
- **Recovery Rate:** How fast the water heater reheats water.
- **BTU:** British Thermal Unit. Measures the heating capacity of gas water heaters.
- **Thermal Expansion:** Water expanding as it heats. Requires an expansion tank in closed systems.

### Drain and Sewer Terms
- **Snaking / Augering:** Using a flexible cable to break through clogs.
- **Hydro-Jetting:** Using high-pressure water to clean the inside of pipes. More thorough than snaking.
- **Root Intrusion:** Tree roots growing into sewer pipes through joints. Very common cause of sewer backups.
- **Bellied Pipe:** A sag or low spot in a sewer line where waste collects.
- **Offset Joint:** When pipe sections shift and no longer align. Causes blockages.
- **Cleanout:** An access point in the sewer line for maintenance and clearing blockages.
- **Camera Inspection:** Sending a fiber-optic camera through pipes to identify problems.
- **Trenchless Repair:** Repairing or replacing sewer lines without digging a trench. Methods include pipe lining (CIPP) and pipe bursting.

### Common Brand Names Customers May Reference
- **Faucets and Fixtures:** Moen, Delta, Kohler, American Standard, Pfister, Grohe, Hansgrohe
- **Toilets:** Kohler, TOTO, American Standard, Glacier Bay (Home Depot brand)
- **Water Heaters:** Rheem, A.O. Smith, Bradford White, Rinnai (tankless), Navien (tankless), Noritz
- **Garbage Disposals:** InSinkErator, Waste King, Moen
- **Water Softeners:** Culligan, Kinetico, GE, Whirlpool, Fleck
- **Sump Pumps:** Zoeller, Wayne, Liberty, Superior Pump

## 5. Common Customer Questions and Ideal Responses

### Pricing Questions

**Q1: "How much does it cost to fix a leaky faucet?"**
A: "Faucet repairs typically range from $75 to $300 depending on the faucet type and what part needs replacing. Our plumber will give you an exact quote before starting any work."

**Q2: "How much is a new water heater?"**
A: "For a standard 40-50 gallon tank water heater, installed, you're typically looking at $800 to $2,200 depending on whether it's gas or electric and the brand. Tankless units run higher, usually $1,500 to $5,000 installed. I can schedule a plumber to evaluate your needs and give you an exact price."

**Q3: "How much to unclog a drain?"**
A: "A single drain clearing typically costs $100 to $350. Main sewer line clearing runs $150 to $600. If the issue is deeper, like root intrusion or a collapsed pipe, additional work may be needed, but the plumber will let you know before proceeding."

**Q4: "Why are plumbers so expensive?"**
A: "Plumbing involves specialized skills, licensing, ongoing training, and liability insurance. Our plumbers are licensed and insured, which protects you and your home. We're transparent about pricing and will always give you a quote before we start."

**Q5: "Do you charge by the hour or by the job?"**
A: "We typically [company-specific - flat rate or hourly]. This means [explain benefit]. You'll know the cost before we begin any work."

### Problem/Diagnostic Questions

**Q6: "My water pressure is low."**
A: "Low water pressure can be caused by several things - a failing pressure reducing valve, corroded galvanized pipes, a partially closed shut-off valve, or even a municipal water issue. Is the low pressure throughout your whole house or just at one fixture?"

**Q7: "My toilet keeps running."**
A: "That's usually a worn-out flapper or a faulty fill valve. Both are relatively inexpensive repairs. You can try jiggling the handle as a temporary fix, but it won't solve the underlying issue. Would you like to schedule a service call?"

**Q8: "My water heater is making popping or rumbling noises."**
A: "That's typically sediment buildup at the bottom of the tank. It's not immediately dangerous, but it reduces efficiency and can shorten the life of the water heater. A flush can often solve it. How old is your water heater?"

**Q9: "I have no hot water."**
A: "Let me ask a few questions. Is your water heater gas or electric? If gas, check if the pilot light is lit. If electric, check the breaker. How old is the unit? If it's older and these basic checks don't fix it, we should send a plumber out."

**Q10: "My drains are all backing up at once."**
A: "If all drains in your home are backing up, that typically points to a main sewer line issue, which is more serious than a single clogged drain. I'd recommend scheduling a service call soon. In the meantime, stop using water in the home to prevent further backup."

**Q11: "I hear a gurgling sound from my drains."**
A: "Gurgling usually indicates a venting issue or a partial blockage in the drain or sewer line. It's not an emergency, but it should be looked at before it becomes a full blockage."

**Q12: "My water bill is unusually high."**
A: "An unexplained spike in your water bill often means a hidden leak - possibly a running toilet, a leaking pipe under the slab, or an outdoor leak you can't see. Our plumber can do a leak detection assessment."

### Scheduling and Logistics

**Q13: "How soon can you come out?"**
A: "For emergencies like flooding, burst pipes, or sewer backups, we prioritize same-day service. For routine issues, we can typically schedule within [company-specific timeframe]. What's the nature of your issue?"

**Q14: "Do you work on weekends?"**
A: "Yes, we offer weekend and after-hours emergency service. Weekend service may carry a slightly higher rate. For non-emergencies, we recommend scheduling during regular business hours for the best pricing."

**Q15: "Can you work on my septic system?"**
A: "We handle plumbing connected to septic systems, but septic tank pumping and maintenance is a separate service. We can refer you to a trusted septic company, or if the issue is with the plumbing inside the home, we can absolutely help."

### Water Quality Questions

**Q16: "Is my water safe to drink?"**
A: "If you're concerned about water quality, we can recommend a water testing service. For filtration solutions, we install whole-house water filters, reverse osmosis systems, and water softeners."

**Q17: "Should I get a tankless water heater?"**
A: "Tankless heaters provide endless hot water and save space, but they cost more upfront and may require gas line or electrical upgrades. They work best for homes with moderate hot water demand. If your family uses a lot of hot water simultaneously, like multiple showers at once, a large tank or multiple tankless units may be needed."

**Q18: "My water smells like rotten eggs."**
A: "A sulfur smell in hot water only usually means the anode rod in your water heater needs replacing. If you smell it from cold water too, it could be a water source issue. If you smell it from drains, it may be a dry P-trap or venting problem. Do not confuse this with a natural gas smell, which is an emergency."

**Q19: "What is the life expectancy of a water heater?"**
A: "Tank water heaters typically last 8 to 12 years. Tankless units can last 15 to 20 years. If yours is over 10 years old and having issues, replacement is often more cost-effective than repair."

**Q20: "Can you install a water heater I bought myself?"**
A: "We can install customer-supplied water heaters. Keep in mind that manufacturer warranties sometimes have conditions about professional installation, and if the unit has any issues, the warranty claim process may differ. We do recommend letting us help you select the right size and type."

### Handling Difficult Situations

**Price shoppers:**
A: "I understand budget is important. Our prices include licensed, insured plumbers, quality parts, and warranty coverage on our work. I'm happy to schedule a visit so you can get an exact quote to compare."

**Angry customer about prior work:**
A: "I apologize for the inconvenience. Let me pull up your account and get our service manager involved to make this right. We stand behind our work."

**Insurance questions:**
A: "We can provide detailed invoices and photos for your insurance claim. The claim process is between you and your insurer, but we're happy to supply whatever documentation they need."

## 6. Licensing and Compliance Notes

### Permits
- **Water heater replacement:** Most jurisdictions require a plumbing permit. The plumber typically handles this.
- **Repiping:** Requires a plumbing permit and often inspection.
- **Sewer line replacement:** Requires permit and inspection. May also need a street/right-of-way permit if work extends to the city main.
- **Gas line work:** Requires a separate gas or plumbing permit. Must be done by a licensed gas fitter.
- **New fixture installation:** Adding new plumbing (not replacing existing) usually requires a permit.
- **Water heater relocation:** If moving to a different location, a permit is required.

### What to Tell Callers About Permits:
- "Our plumber handles all necessary permits. The cost is typically included in the quote."
- "Permits ensure the work is done to code and passes inspection, which protects your home's value and your family's safety."

### Common Code Requirements:
- Water heaters require a temperature and pressure (T&P) relief valve and proper drain line
- Gas water heaters need proper venting and combustion air
- Anti-scald valves are required on showers in most jurisdictions (set to max 120F)
- Backflow prevention devices required where cross-contamination is possible
- Shut-off valves required at each fixture
- Minimum pipe sizes specified by code for different fixture types
- Cleanouts required at certain intervals on sewer lines

## 7. Upsell and Cross-Sell Awareness

### Natural Add-On Opportunities:

**During a Drain Cleaning Call:**
- Camera inspection: "While we have the line open, our plumber can run a camera through to check the overall condition of your pipes. This helps catch potential problems early."
- Drain maintenance plan: "We offer annual drain maintenance plans that help prevent clogs from building up."

**During a Water Heater Call:**
- Expansion tank: "If you don't have one, an expansion tank protects your water heater and plumbing from thermal expansion pressure."
- Water softener: "Hard water is one of the main reasons water heaters fail early. A water softener can extend the life of your new unit."
- Anode rod replacement: Mention during water heater flush

**During Any Service Call:**
- Maintenance plan: "We offer a plumbing maintenance plan that includes annual inspections and priority scheduling."
- Shut-off valve upgrades: "If your shut-off valves are the older gate-valve type, upgrading to ball valves makes them more reliable in an emergency."

**How to Mention Without Being Pushy:**
- Frame as prevention: "This is something that can save you money down the road."
- Let the plumber handle detailed recommendations on-site
- The phone agent's job is to book the appointment, not sell add-ons

## 8. Red Flags and Liability Avoidance

### NEVER Say:
- "It's probably just a [specific diagnosis]." (You cannot diagnose plumbing issues over the phone.)
- "That will cost exactly $X." (Always provide ranges.)
- "Your water is safe." (You cannot assess water quality over the phone.)
- "You can fix that yourself." (Liability issue, especially for gas or sewer work.)
- "Just pour some Drano down there." (Chemical drain cleaners can damage pipes and create safety hazards.)
- "That sounds like it could be a slab leak." (Do not alarm callers with worst-case scenarios before diagnosis.)
- "Your insurance should cover that." (You cannot speak for their insurer.)

### Phrases That Create Liability:
- "I promise we'll fix it today." (Use "We'll do our best to resolve it as quickly as possible.")
- "It's definitely not a big deal." (Use "Our plumber will evaluate and let you know exactly what's going on.")
- "You won't need a permit for that." (Use "Our plumber will handle any permits that are required.")
- "That pipe should last forever." (No pipe lasts forever.)

### Handling Threats:
**"I'm going to report you to the BBB / state licensing board":**
A: "I take your concerns seriously and want to resolve this. Let me connect you with our service manager who can review the situation."

**"I'm going to sue / calling my lawyer":**
A: "I'm sorry to hear that. For any legal matters, please direct correspondence to [company address]. I'd still like to try to resolve your concern - can I have a manager call you?"

**General Rule:** Stay calm, don't argue, don't admit fault, escalate to management immediately.
