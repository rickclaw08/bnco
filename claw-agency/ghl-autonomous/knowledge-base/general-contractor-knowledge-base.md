## 2. Emergency Protocols

### TRUE EMERGENCIES - Tell caller to call 911 FIRST, then call us back:
- Structural collapse (wall, floor, ceiling, or roof giving way)
- Gas line hit or broken during construction or renovation
- Exposed or damaged electrical wiring with sparking or fire risk
- Active fire in the home or on the construction site
- Flooding with electrical hazard (water reaching outlets, panels, or wiring)
- Someone injured on a job site or during a home project

### URGENT (Same-Day/Next-Day Service Needed):
- Water pipe burst during renovation, active flooding
- Load-bearing wall or structural element showing signs of failure (cracking, bowing, sagging)
- Roof or exterior wall breach leaving the home exposed to weather
- Foundation crack actively widening or with water intrusion
- Electrical issue discovered during work (exposed wires, tripped main breaker)
- Sewage backup exposed during renovation
- Security breach (exterior door or window removed during work and cannot be secured)

### ROUTINE (Schedule Within Normal Business Hours):
- General remodeling estimates and consultations
- Permit questions and project planning
- Cosmetic repairs (drywall, paint, trim)
- Non-urgent foundation cracks (hairline, stable)
- Fence or deck repairs
- Project timeline questions for in-progress work
- Warranty or callback requests on completed work
- Second opinion on another contractor's estimate

### Triage Questions to Ask:
1. "Is anyone in immediate danger or injured?" (If yes, tell them to call 911)
2. "Is there active water, gas, or electrical damage happening right now?"
3. "What type of project are you calling about?" (Repair, remodel, addition, new build)
4. "Is this an existing project in progress or a new request?"
5. "Have you had any permits pulled for this work?"
6. "What is the approximate scope and budget you have in mind?"
7. "When were you hoping to start the project?"
8. "Is this for your primary residence, rental property, or commercial space?"
9. "Have you gotten other estimates?"
10. "Is this an insurance claim?"

### Safety Instructions for Callers:
- Water pipe burst during renovation: "Shut off the main water supply immediately. If the water is near any electrical outlets or the breaker panel, do not walk through it. Turn off the main breaker from a dry location if possible. Then call us."
- Gas line damaged: "Evacuate the house immediately. Do not turn on or off any lights or appliances. Call 911 from outside, then call the gas company. Call us after you are safe."
- Structural concern: "Do not go into the affected area. If you hear cracking sounds or see walls or floors shifting, get everyone out of that section of the house. We will come assess it."
- Electrical issue: "Do not touch any exposed wires or attempt repairs. Turn off the breaker to that area if you can safely reach the panel. We will coordinate with a licensed electrician."
- In-progress project concern: "If you feel unsafe about anything on the job site, keep everyone out of the area until we can send someone to evaluate."

- **Summer (June-August):** Peak pricing and longest wait times. Material costs may also spike due to demand.
- **Fall (September-October):** Good balance of availability and weather. Last chance for outdoor work in northern states.

## 4. Industry Terminology Glossary

### Project Types
- **Remodel:** Changing the structure, layout, or function of an existing space. Moving walls, rerouting plumbing, or changing the floor plan.
- **Renovation:** Updating or restoring an existing space without changing the layout. New finishes, fixtures, and cosmetic improvements.
- **Restoration:** Returning a space to its original condition. Common in historic homes.
- **Addition:** New construction that increases the home's total living area by building outward or upward.
- **Extension:** Expanding an existing room, typically by moving an exterior wall outward.
- **Bump-Out:** A small extension (usually 2-4 feet) of one wall to add space to a room.
- **ADU (Accessory Dwelling Unit):** A secondary housing unit on a single-family lot. Can be attached or detached. Also called in-law suite, granny flat, or casita.
- **Gut Renovation:** Stripping a space down to the studs and rebuilding everything.
- **Cosmetic Renovation:** Surface-level updates (paint, hardware, fixtures) without structural changes.
- **New Construction:** Building a structure from the ground up on vacant land or after a demolition.
- **Custom Home:** A home built to unique specifications and plans, not from a standard template.
- **Spec Home:** A home built by a builder without a specific buyer, designed to appeal to the general market.

### Construction Terms
- **Load-Bearing Wall:** A wall that supports the weight of the structure above it. Cannot be removed without installing a beam or header.
- **Non-Load-Bearing Wall (Partition Wall):** A wall that serves only as a room divider. Can be removed without structural impact.
- **Header/Beam:** A horizontal structural member that spans an opening (door, window, removed wall) and transfers weight to the sides.
- **LVL (Laminated Veneer Lumber):** An engineered wood beam. Stronger than solid lumber, used for headers and beams.
- **Stud:** Vertical framing members (typically 2x4 or 2x6) that form the skeleton of walls.
- **Joist:** Horizontal framing members that support floors and ceilings.
- **Subfloor:** The structural layer (plywood or OSB) over the joists, beneath the finished floor.
- **Slab:** A concrete foundation poured directly on the ground. No basement or crawlspace.
- **Crawlspace:** A low, unfinished area under the house, typically 1-4 feet high.
- **Footing:** The widened base of a foundation wall that distributes the structure's weight into the soil.
- **Pier:** A vertical support column, typically concrete, that supports a beam or structure.
- **R-Value:** A measure of insulation's ability to resist heat flow. Higher is better.
- **Vapor Barrier:** A sheet of plastic or foil that prevents moisture from passing through walls, floors, or ceilings.

### Trade Terminology
- **Rough-In:** The first phase of plumbing, electrical, or HVAC installation where pipes, wires, and ducts are run before walls are closed up.
- **Trim-Out (Finish):** The final phase where fixtures, outlets, switches, and visible components are installed.
- **Punch List:** A list of minor items to complete or fix at the end of a construction project before final payment.
- **Change Order:** A formal document modifying the original contract scope, schedule, or price. Common and expected in construction.
- **Scope of Work (SOW):** A detailed description of all the work to be performed in a contract.
- **Allowance:** A budget placeholder in a contract for items not yet selected (e.g., "lighting allowance: $2,000"). The homeowner picks the specific products later.
- **Draw Schedule:** A payment plan that releases funds at specific project milestones (e.g., 10% at contract signing, 30% at rough-in, etc.).
- **Retainage:** A percentage of payment (usually 5%-10%) held back until the project is 100% complete and approved.
- **GC (General Contractor):** The primary contractor responsible for managing the project, hiring subcontractors, and coordinating all work.
- **Sub (Subcontractor):** A specialist (plumber, electrician, roofer, etc.) hired by the GC to perform specific trade work.
- **Bid:** A contractor's formal proposal with pricing for a defined scope of work.
- **Estimate:** A rough cost projection, less formal than a bid.
- **Lien Waiver:** A document from a contractor or sub waiving their right to place a lien on the property, typically signed upon receiving payment.
- **Mechanic's Lien:** A legal claim against a property by a contractor or supplier who has not been paid for work or materials.

### Materials
- **Pressure-Treated Lumber:** Wood chemically treated to resist rot and insects. Standard for outdoor structures (decks, fences, framing near ground).
- **LVP (Luxury Vinyl Plank):** A waterproof, durable flooring that mimics hardwood. Very popular in renovations.
- **Quartz:** An engineered stone countertop material. Consistent color, non-porous, low maintenance.
- **Granite:** A natural stone countertop. Each slab is unique. Requires periodic sealing.
- **Cement Board (Hardie Board):** Fiber cement material used for siding and tile backer board. Rot-proof and fire-resistant.
- **Drywall (Sheetrock):** Gypsum panels used for interior walls and ceilings. Standard is 1/2-inch; 5/8-inch is used for fire rating and soundproofing.
- **Green Board:** Moisture-resistant drywall used in bathrooms and kitchens.
- **OSB (Oriented Strand Board):** Engineered wood panel used for sheathing, subflooring, and roof decking.
- **Plywood:** Layered wood panel. Used for subflooring, sheathing, and cabinets.
- **PEX:** Flexible plastic piping used for water supply lines. Replaced copper in many modern homes.
- **Romex:** Brand name for NM (non-metallic) sheathed electrical cable used in residential wiring.

### Permits and Inspections
- **Building Permit:** Authorization from the local government to proceed with construction. Required for most structural, electrical, plumbing, and mechanical work.
- **Certificate of Occupancy (CO):** A document from the local government certifying that a building meets all codes and is safe to inhabit.
- **Rough Inspection:** An inspection of work before walls are closed up (framing, rough plumbing, rough electrical).
- **Final Inspection:** The last inspection after all work is complete. Required before a CO is issued.
- **Variance:** Permission to deviate from zoning rules (setbacks, height limits, etc.).
- **Setback:** The required minimum distance between a structure and the property line.
- **Easement:** A legal right allowing someone else to use a portion of your property (e.g., utility companies).

### Common Professional Roles Customers May Reference
- **Architect:** Designs buildings and creates construction drawings. Required for large additions and new construction in most areas.
- **Structural Engineer:** Evaluates and designs the load-bearing components. Required when removing walls, adding stories, or addressing foundation issues.
- **Interior Designer:** Plans the aesthetics, layout, and finishes of interior spaces. Different from an architect.
- **Building Inspector:** A government official who inspects construction work for code compliance.
- **Project Manager (PM):** Oversees the day-to-day management of the construction project. May be the GC or a separate hire.

## 5. Common Customer Questions and Ideal Responses

### Pricing Questions

**Q1: "How much does a kitchen remodel cost?"**
A: "Kitchen remodels typically range from $10,000 for a minor refresh up to $65,000 or more for a major renovation. The national average is about $27,000. Cost depends on the size of the kitchen, materials you choose, whether you are changing the layout, and the level of finishes. We can schedule a consultation to understand your goals and provide a detailed estimate."

**Q2: "How much does a bathroom remodel cost?"**
A: "Bathroom remodels typically range from about $6,600 to $17,600, with the national average around $12,100. A half-bath refresh can start as low as $1,500, while a primary bathroom with custom tile and premium fixtures can reach $30,000. It depends on the scope. Would you like to schedule a consultation?"

**Q3: "How much do you charge as a general contractor?"**
A: "General contractor fees are typically 10% to 25% of the total project cost, depending on the scope and complexity. For smaller projects, we may work on a flat fee or hourly basis. The fee covers project management, subcontractor coordination, scheduling, permitting, and quality oversight. We will provide a transparent breakdown in your estimate."

**Q4: "Can you give me a ballpark over the phone?"**
A: "I can give you a general range based on what you describe, but an accurate estimate requires an on-site visit. Every home and project has unique factors like existing conditions, accessibility, and material choices that affect the price. Our consultations are free, and there is no obligation."

**Q5: "Why is your estimate higher than the other contractor?"**
A: "Great question. I would encourage you to compare what is included in each estimate. Does it include permits, cleanup, debris disposal, and a warranty? Are the materials specified or left vague? Does the other contractor carry proper insurance and workers' comp? Our estimates are fully detailed and include everything. We would rather be upfront than surprise you with extras later."

### Project Planning Questions

**Q6: "How long will my project take?"**
A: "Timeline depends on the project scope. A bathroom remodel typically takes 2 to 4 weeks. A kitchen remodel takes 4 to 8 weeks. A home addition can take 3 to 6 months. We will provide a detailed timeline in your proposal, and we communicate proactively if anything changes."

**Q7: "Do I need a permit for this?"**
A: "Most structural, electrical, plumbing, and mechanical work requires a permit. Simple cosmetic updates like painting and hardware swaps usually do not. We handle all permitting as part of our service and include the cost in your estimate. Pulling the right permits protects you and ensures the work is inspected for code compliance."

**Q8: "Can I live in my house during the renovation?"**
A: "In most cases, yes, though it depends on the project. A kitchen remodel means you will be without a functional kitchen for several weeks. A bathroom remodel is manageable if you have another bathroom available. Large-scale gut renovations or work involving asbestos or lead paint may require you to temporarily relocate. We will advise you based on your specific project."

**Q9: "What is the process from start to finish?"**
A: "Typically it goes: initial consultation, design and material selections, proposal and contract signing, permit application, construction (demo, rough-in, inspections, finish work), final walkthrough and punch list, final inspection and sign-off. We keep you informed at every stage."

**Q10: "Should I hire a general contractor or manage the subs myself?"**
A: "If you have construction experience and time to manage multiple trades, scheduling, inspections, and problem-solving, you can save the GC fee by managing it yourself. Most homeowners hire a GC because we handle all of that complexity. We know which subs are reliable, we manage the schedule, and we are responsible for the end result. It also protects you from liability."

### Material and Design Questions

**Q11: "What countertop material do you recommend?"**
A: "Quartz is the most popular choice right now because it is durable, non-porous, and low-maintenance. Granite is a classic natural stone option. Laminate is the most budget-friendly. Butcher block is great for a warm look. We can show you samples and discuss what fits your lifestyle and budget."

**Q12: "What flooring is best for a kitchen/bathroom?"**
A: "Luxury vinyl plank (LVP) is extremely popular because it is waterproof, durable, affordable, and looks like real wood. Tile is the traditional choice for bathrooms and offers the most design options. Engineered hardwood works well in kitchens but is not ideal for bathrooms. We can walk you through options during the design phase."

**Q13: "How do I choose cabinets?"**
A: "Cabinets come in three tiers: stock (pre-made, most affordable, limited styles), semi-custom (some modifications allowed, mid-range), and custom (built to your exact specifications, highest cost). Stock cabinets run $100 to $300 per linear foot while custom can reach $500 to $1,200. For most homeowners, semi-custom offers the best balance of choice and value."

### Scheduling and Logistics Questions

**Q14: "How soon can you start?"**
A: "That depends on the time of year and our current workload. During peak season (spring through fall), we are often booked several weeks to months out. For interior projects in winter, we may have faster availability. The sooner you reach out, the sooner we can get you on the schedule."

**Q15: "Do you offer financing?"**
A: "Yes, we offer financing options for larger projects. [Company-specific details.] Our team can go over options during the estimate or consultation. Many homeowners also use home equity lines of credit (HELOCs) or home improvement loans."

**Q16: "What happens if there is a problem after the job is done?"**
A: "We provide a workmanship warranty on all our projects. [Company-specific duration and terms.] If something we installed has an issue, we come back and fix it. We also do a detailed walkthrough at the end of every project to catch anything before we wrap up."

**Q17: "Do I need to hire an architect?"**
A: "For cosmetic renovations and most standard remodels, no. For additions, new construction, or anything that changes the structural footprint of the home, many jurisdictions require stamped architectural drawings. We work with architects regularly and can coordinate that for you if needed."

### Emergency and Problem Questions

**Q18: "I found mold during my renovation."**
A: "Mold should be handled by a certified mold remediation specialist before we continue construction. We will pause work in the affected area, assess the extent, and coordinate with a remediation company. This is a common discovery in older homes and we know how to manage it."

**Q19: "My contractor disappeared mid-project. Can you take over?"**
A: "We do take over projects started by other contractors, though it requires a thorough evaluation first. We need to inspect the work that has been done, verify permits, and assess what remains. There may be corrections needed to bring the existing work up to our standards and code compliance. We will give you an honest assessment."

**Q20: "The cost is going over budget. What can I do?"**
A: "Cost overruns happen, often due to unforeseen conditions like hidden water damage, outdated wiring, or structural issues. We document everything with change orders so there are no surprises. If budget is a concern, we can help you prioritize, adjust material selections, or phase the project. Our goal is to deliver the best result within your means."

**Q21: "We found asbestos/lead paint. Now what?"**
A: "Both asbestos and lead paint require certified professionals for safe removal. We will pause work, bring in a testing company to confirm, and coordinate with licensed abatement contractors. This is especially common in homes built before 1980. The cost varies, but safety comes first."

**Q22: "How do I know if a wall is load-bearing before I knock it out?"**
A: "Do not remove any wall without professional assessment. Load-bearing walls support the structure above them, and removing one without proper support can cause serious damage or collapse. We evaluate every wall before removal and bring in a structural engineer when needed. This is included in our planning process."

**Q23: "I want to add a second story. Is that possible?"**
A: "In many cases, yes, but it is one of the most complex renovation projects. It requires structural engineering to evaluate whether your foundation and existing framing can support the added weight. You will also need architectural plans, permits, and likely need to relocate temporarily. Costs typically start around $100,000. We can do a feasibility assessment."

### Handling Difficult Situations

**Angry customer about project delays:**
A: "I understand how frustrating delays are. Let me connect you with your project manager so we can review the timeline and give you an updated completion date. We want to keep you informed every step of the way."

**Customer questioning quality of work:**
A: "Your satisfaction is our priority. Let me schedule a time for our project manager to walk through the concerns with you on-site. If anything does not meet our standards, we will address it."

**Sticker shock after receiving estimate:**
A: "I understand it is a significant investment. We can look at ways to adjust the scope or materials to bring the cost closer to your budget. Phasing the project is also an option. Let us find the right approach for you."

## 6. Licensing and Compliance Notes

### Permits
- **Structural work:** Additions, wall removal/modification, foundation work, and structural changes require building permits.
- **Electrical work:** Any new circuits, panel upgrades, or rewiring requires an electrical permit and licensed electrician.
- **Plumbing work:** Moving or adding fixtures, rerouting pipes, and water heater installation require plumbing permits.
- **Mechanical (HVAC):** New installations, duct modifications, and system replacements require mechanical permits.
- **Demolition:** Full or partial demolition typically requires a demolition permit.
- **Cosmetic work:** Painting, hardware swaps, and similar surface-level work generally do not require permits.

### What to Tell Callers About Permits:
- "We handle all permit applications and inspections as part of our project management. The permit fees are included in your estimate."
- "Pulling permits protects you. It ensures all work is inspected for safety and code compliance, and it avoids problems when you sell your home."
- If a homeowner wants to skip permits: "We understand the desire to save, but unpermitted work can create serious issues - from safety risks to problems with insurance claims, resale, and financing. We always work with permits."

### Common Code and Compliance Topics:
- Minimum ceiling heights (typically 7 feet for habitable rooms, may be lower for basements)
- Egress requirements for bedrooms (minimum window size for emergency exit)
- Smoke and carbon monoxide detector requirements
- Stair code (rise, run, handrails, guardrails)
- ADA compliance requirements for commercial or aging-in-place projects
- Energy code compliance (insulation R-values, window efficiency ratings)
- Setback requirements for additions and new structures
- HOA restrictions (design review boards may require approval)
- Historic district regulations (may limit exterior modifications)

### Contractor Licensing Notes:
- General contractor licensing requirements vary by state. Some states require a state license; others regulate at the county or city level.
- Workers' compensation insurance is required in most states for contractors with employees.
- General liability insurance (typically $1M-$2M) is standard and should be verified by homeowners.
- Surety bonds may be required for larger projects or in certain states.
- Specialty trade work (electrical, plumbing, HVAC) requires separate licenses held by the subcontractors.
- Always verify: license number, insurance certificates, bond status, and references.

- Electrical upgrades: "If your home has older wiring, we can upgrade outlets to GFCI and add more circuits while the walls are open."
- Pantry addition or expansion: "We often find ways to create or expand pantry space during a kitchen remodel."
- Flooring extension: "Would you like the new flooring to extend into the adjacent dining area or hallway for a seamless look?"

**During a Bathroom Remodel:**
- Heated floors: "Radiant floor heating adds about $500 to $1,500 and makes a huge difference in comfort, especially in winter."
- Ventilation fan upgrade: "A new exhaust fan with humidity sensing prevents mold and moisture damage."
- Aging-in-place features: "If you are thinking long-term, grab bars, a curbless shower, and wider doorways are easy to add now."

**During an Addition:**
- Upgraded insulation: "Since we are building from scratch, going above minimum insulation requirements saves on energy bills for years."
- Prewiring: "We can run wiring for speakers, networking, or future technology while the walls are open. It is a fraction of the cost compared to doing it later."
- Smart home integration: "Smart switches, thermostat zones, and security prewiring are easy to add during construction."

**During Any Project:**
- Maintenance plan: "We offer annual maintenance check-ins to catch small issues before they become big ones."
- Adjacent room refresh: "Since we are already mobilized, we can handle smaller projects in adjacent areas at a reduced mobilization cost."
- Exterior improvements: "If you are adding value inside, consider matching it outside with new siding, landscaping, or a deck."

**How to Mention Without Being Pushy:**
- Frame it as timing: "Since we already have the walls open..." or "This is the most cost-effective time to..."
- Connect it to value: "This adds about $X now, but would cost 2-3 times more as a standalone project later."
- Let the project manager handle the detailed conversation. The phone agent's job is to book the consultation.

## 8. Red Flags and Liability Avoidance

### NEVER Say:
- "That will cost exactly $X." (Always give ranges and clarify that the estimator provides exact pricing after an on-site assessment.)
- "You do not need a permit for that." (Permit requirements vary and this creates legal exposure.)
- "We can start next week" without checking the actual schedule. (Overpromising on timelines damages trust.)
- "That other contractor did terrible work." (Never disparage competitors. Focus on your own value.)
- "Your house just needs..." (Do not diagnose structural, electrical, or plumbing issues over the phone.)
- "It will definitely be done by [date]." (Construction timelines shift. Use "target" or "estimated" completion dates.)
- "We guarantee the lowest price." (Unless the company has a documented price-match policy.)
- "You do not need an engineer for that." (Structural assessments require professional evaluation.)

### Phrases That Create Liability:
- "I promise it will be done by..." (Use "our target completion date is..." instead)
- "That is an easy fix." (Use "our team will evaluate and provide a plan" instead)
- "You should tear that wall out yourself to save money." (Never advise DIY structural work)
- "Your foundation looks fine from what you describe." (Use "we would need to inspect it in person" instead)
- "That is up to code." (Only inspectors make that determination)

### Red Flags About Clients (for internal awareness):
- Requesting work without permits to save money
- Asking contractors to write false insurance claims or inflated invoices
- Wanting to pay entirely in cash with no contract
- Refusing to sign a contract or change orders
- Scope creep without willingness to adjust budget
- History of disputes with multiple previous contractors

### Handling Threats:
**"I am going to call the BBB / leave a bad review":**
A: "I understand your frustration, and I want to help resolve this. Let me connect you with our project manager who can address your concerns directly. We value our reputation and take every concern seriously."

**"I am going to sue / I am calling my lawyer":**
A: "I am sorry you feel that way. I would like to try to resolve this for you. Let me have our manager reach out to discuss the situation. For legal correspondence, please have your attorney contact us at [company address/phone]."

**"I am not paying until everything is perfect":**
A: "We want you to be completely satisfied. Let us schedule a walkthrough to go through your concerns item by item. Our contract includes a punch list process specifically for this. We will address every legitimate item."

**General Rule:** Stay calm, empathetic, and professional. Do not argue, get defensive, or make admissions of fault. Escalate to a project manager. Document everything in writing.
