## 1. Purpose

This memo explains why the contractor classification model used in this packet carries inherent legal risk, and what ClawOps leadership must monitor to reduce that risk in practice.

Signing a contract that says "independent contractor" does not make someone an independent contractor. Federal and state agencies, courts, and tax authorities look at the actual working relationship. If ClawOps treats contractors like employees in practice, the contracts will not override reality.

## 2. What This Packet Assumes

This onboarding packet is built on the assumption that each contractor:

- Chooses when, where, and how to perform outbound sales activities
- Provides their own equipment, workspace, and internet access
- Is free to work for other companies simultaneously
- Bears their own business expenses
- Is responsible for their own federal, state, and local taxes
- Receives no salary, hourly wage, benefits, or guaranteed compensation
- Is paid only by commission on closed deals as defined in the Commission Exhibit

If the actual working relationship departs from these assumptions, the classification may not hold.

## 3. What Makes a Contractor Look Like an Employee

The following operational habits increase misclassification risk. ClawOps leadership should monitor for and avoid these patterns:

**Schedule Control**

- Requiring contractors to work specific hours or shifts
- Tracking clock-in and clock-out times
- Penalizing contractors for not being available during set windows
- Requiring attendance at mandatory daily or weekly meetings with no legitimate compliance or training purpose

**Method Control**

- Dictating exactly how the contractor must conduct sales conversations beyond approved compliance messaging
- Requiring contractors to follow rigid call-by-call workflows that leave no room for independent judgment
- Requiring prior approval for routine sales decisions that a reasonable independent businessperson would make on their own

**Economic Dependence**

- Structuring compensation so that the contractor is effectively dependent on ClawOps as their sole income source
- Restricting the contractor's ability to offer similar services to others
- Providing all leads, all tools, and all infrastructure such that the contractor has no meaningful investment of their own

**Integration**

- Giving contractors company email addresses, business cards, or titles that suggest employment
- Including contractors in employee-only meetings, performance reviews, or HR processes
- Applying progressive discipline (verbal warning, written warning, suspension) instead of contract-based remedies

**Benefits and Reimbursements**

- Reimbursing contractors for business expenses (mileage, phone, software)
- Offering health insurance, paid time off, retirement contributions, or similar benefits
- Providing equipment purchased and owned by ClawOps for the contractor's dedicated use

## 4. What ClawOps Can Do Without Increasing Risk

ClawOps may lawfully:

- Require compliance with applicable telemarketing laws, DNC rules, and recording consent requirements
- Require use of approved scripts for legal compliance and brand integrity purposes
- Require use of company CRM and calling platforms for data security and audit trail purposes
- Require confidentiality and non-disclosure of proprietary information
- Require non-solicitation of company clients and workforce for a reasonable duration
- Set quality standards for client-facing communications
- Provide training on company products, services, pricing, and compliance requirements
- Remove system access for cause
- Terminate the contractor relationship per the terms of the agreement

The distinction is between controlling **what must be achieved and what rules must be followed** (permissible) versus controlling **how and when the contractor performs their daily work** (risky).

## 5. When to Pause and Get Legal Review

ClawOps should consult legal counsel before:

- Onboarding contractors in a new state (classification standards vary by jurisdiction)
- Changing compensation structure in a way that increases economic dependence
- Requiring contractors to use ClawOps-provided equipment exclusively
- Implementing any tracking, monitoring, or scheduling system beyond CRM usage
- Converting a contractor to employee status (see File 18)
- Responding to any government inquiry about worker classification
- Receiving any claim or complaint from a contractor alleging employment status

## 6. Federal and State Standards

Worker classification is evaluated differently depending on the authority:

- **IRS:** Uses a multi-factor common law test focused on behavioral control, financial control, and relationship type
- **DOL (Fair Labor Standards Act):** Uses an "economic reality" test focused on economic dependence
- **State agencies:** Many states have their own tests, some stricter than federal standards (e.g., California's ABC test, Massachusetts' presumption of employment, New Jersey's ABC test)
- **State unemployment and workers' compensation agencies:** May apply separate tests

A contractor classified correctly under federal IRS rules may still be classified as an employee under a particular state's test.

## 7. Difference Between This Packet and W-2 Onboarding

| Element | 1099 Contractor Packet | W-2 Employee Onboarding |
|---------|----------------------|------------------------|
| Tax forms | W-9 | W-4, I-9 |
| Compensation | Commission per exhibit | Salary, hourly, or commission with minimum wage floor |
| Benefits | None | As required by law and company policy |
| Withholding | None (contractor responsible) | Federal, state, FICA, Medicare |
| Workers' comp | Not provided (unless required) | Required in most states |
| Unemployment | Not applicable | Employer pays into state fund |
| Schedule | Contractor controls | Employer may set |
| Termination | Per contract terms | Subject to employment law |
