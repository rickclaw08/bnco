## 1. PURPOSE

This memo provides guidance and warnings for ClawOps leadership in the event that the company considers transitioning from a 1099 independent contractor model to hiring W-2 employees. This memo is for internal planning purposes only and does not constitute legal advice.

## 2. THIS PACKET IS NOT FOR EMPLOYEES

**The ClawOps Contractor Onboarding Packet (Files 01-20) is designed exclusively for 1099 independent contractors.** None of the documents in this packet are suitable for use with W-2 employees. If ClawOps hires employees, a completely separate set of employment documents must be created.

Do not attempt to re-title, modify, or repurpose contractor agreements for employee use. The legal requirements, regulatory obligations, and liability exposures are fundamentally different.

---

## 3. WHAT CHANGES IF CLAWOPS HIRES EMPLOYEES

If ClawOps transitions to a W-2 employee model for any role, the following obligations arise:

### 3.1 New Documentation Required
- Employee offer letter or employment agreement (separate from the ICA)
- Employee handbook covering workplace policies, anti-harassment, anti-discrimination, leave policies, and disciplinary procedures
- At-will employment acknowledgment (if applicable in the employee's state)
- Employee confidentiality and IP assignment agreement (separate from File 05)
- Employee non-solicitation agreement (separate from File 06, must comply with stricter employee non-compete laws)

### 3.2 Tax and Payroll Obligations
- **Federal income tax withholding** (W-4 form required)
- **State income tax withholding** (where applicable)
- **FICA taxes** (Social Security: 6.2% employer share, Medicare: 1.45% employer share)
- **Federal Unemployment Tax (FUTA)**
- **State Unemployment Tax (SUTA)**
- **W-2 forms** issued annually (replacing 1099-NEC)
- Payroll service or software required for compliant withholding and reporting

### 3.3 Employment Forms Required
- **W-4** (Employee's Withholding Certificate)
- **I-9** (Employment Eligibility Verification, with supporting documentation)
- State-specific new hire reporting forms
- Direct deposit authorization

### 3.4 Federal Labor Law Compliance
- **Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA):** Minimum wage, overtime (1.5x for hours over 40/week for non-exempt employees), recordkeeping
- **Equal Employment Opportunity (EEO):** Anti-discrimination compliance
- **Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA):** If 50+ employees within 75 miles
- **Occupational Safety and Health Act (OSHA):** Workplace safety

### 3.5 Insurance and Benefits
- **Workers' compensation insurance** (required in Ohio and most states)
- **Unemployment insurance contributions**
- **Affordable Care Act (ACA) compliance:** If 50+ full-time equivalents, must offer qualifying health coverage or face penalties
- Review of voluntary benefit obligations (health, dental, vision, retirement)

## 4. WHY EMPLOYEE COMPENSATION PLANS NEED DIFFERENT WORDING

### 4.1 Commission-Only Without Minimum Wage Floor Violates FLSA

The current contractor commission structure (15% of one-time Setup Fee, no guaranteed minimum) **cannot be used as-is for employees.** Under the FLSA:

- Employees must be paid at least the federal minimum wage ($7.25/hour as of this writing, or higher if state/local minimums apply) for all hours worked
- Ohio minimum wage may exceed the federal floor (currently $10.45/hour, adjusted annually)
- If commission earnings in a pay period fall below minimum wage for hours worked, the employer must make up the difference ("draw against commission" or "minimum wage guarantee")

### 4.2 Overtime Rules Apply

- Non-exempt employees must receive 1.5x their regular rate for hours worked over 40 per week
- Commission earnings are factored into the regular rate calculation for overtime purposes
- The "outside sales" exemption may apply to some sales employees, but it has strict criteria (work must be primarily performed away from the employer's place of business)

### 4.3 Prohibited Language

The following language from the current contractor packet **must not appear** in employee compensation plans:
- "No guaranteed minimum compensation"
- "Contractor is solely responsible for own taxes"
- "No benefits"
- "Contractor controls how, when, and where work is performed" (if the company actually controls these for employees)

## 5. WHY WAGE LAW REVIEW IS ESSENTIAL

### 5.1 State-by-State Variation

Commission payment laws vary significantly by state:
- Some states require written commission agreements with specific disclosures
- Some states require commission payment within a specific number of days after the commission is "earned"
- Some states have different rules for commission payment upon termination vs. during employment

### 5.2 Termination Pay Rules

- **California:** All earned commissions must be paid upon termination (even if not yet "cleared")
- **New York:** Earned commissions are considered wages and must be paid on the next regular payday
- **Illinois:** Earned commissions must be paid within 13 days of the end of the pay period
- **Ohio:** Earned commissions are generally treated as wages; late payment may trigger penalties

### 5.3 Triple Damages and Penalties

In many states, earned commissions are treated as "wages" under state wage payment statutes. Failure to timely pay earned commissions can result in:
- **Treble (3x) damages** in some states (e.g., Massachusetts, Connecticut)
- **Waiting time penalties** (e.g., California: up to 30 days of wages)
- **Liquidated damages** under federal FLSA (equal to unpaid wages)
- Attorney's fees and costs awarded to the employee

### 5.4 Misclassification Penalties

If ClawOps currently classifies workers as independent contractors but a court or agency determines they are employees, the company may face:
- Back payment of all employment taxes (employer share of FICA, FUTA, SUTA)
- Back payment of minimum wage and overtime for all hours worked
- Penalties and interest from the IRS and state tax agencies
- Back payment of benefits that should have been provided
- State-level misclassification fines (Ohio: up to $5,000 per violation under certain statutes)
- Personal liability for responsible officers in some cases

## 6. RECOMMENDED STEPS BEFORE ANY CONVERSION

If ClawOps decides to hire W-2 employees in any capacity:

1. **Retain an Ohio-licensed employment attorney** to review the employment relationship structure and draft compliant employment documents

2. **Create a separate employee handbook** covering all workplace policies, benefits, leave, anti-harassment, anti-discrimination, and disciplinary procedures

3. **Set up a payroll service** (e.g., Gusto, ADP, Paychex) for compliant tax withholding, reporting, and payment

4. **Review state-specific requirements** for each state where employees will work (minimum wage, commission payment timing, termination pay rules, required disclosures)

5. **Obtain workers' compensation insurance** for all states where employees work

6. **Do NOT simply re-title contractor agreements as employee agreements.** The entire document suite must be rebuilt from scratch with employee-appropriate language, protections, and compliance provisions.

7. **Conduct an internal classification audit** to ensure all current workers are correctly classified before adding employees to the workforce

## 7. SUMMARY

This contractor onboarding packet is built for 1099 independent contractors. The compensation structure, control provisions, tax provisions, and benefits disclaimers are all designed for that relationship. Converting to a W-2 model requires a ground-up rebuild of the documentation, new legal compliance infrastructure, and professional legal counsel.

**Do not attempt to use this packet for employees.**
